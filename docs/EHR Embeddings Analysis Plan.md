# **Enhancing Clinical Risk Prediction: A Literature Review of Advanced Large Language Model Techniques and Hybrid Models**

## **Section 1: Introduction to Clinical Risk Prediction with Electronic Health Record (EHR) Data**

### **1.1. The Importance of Accurate Clinical Risk Prediction in Healthcare**

Accurate clinical risk prediction plays a pivotal role in modern healthcare, enabling proactive patient management, optimizing resource allocation, and ultimately improving patient outcomes.1 Early identification of patients at high risk for adverse events allows healthcare providers to implement timely preventive interventions and tailor care strategies, which can be crucial in strategic hospital decision-making.4 The advent of Electronic Health Records (EHRs) has provided an unprecedented opportunity for developing and validating sophisticated risk prediction models. EHRs, as comprehensive digital repositories of patient health information, contain vast amounts of longitudinal data from large and diverse patient populations, reflecting real-world clinical practice.1 According to Goldstein et al., risk scores developed from EHRs often utilize data from tens of thousands, and in some cases, over one hundred thousand individuals, far exceeding the sample sizes typically available in individual trials or registries.1

The integration of these risk prediction models directly into EHR systems further enhances their clinical utility by making risk assessments readily available to clinicians at the point of care, thereby obviating the need for manual risk calculation and facilitating more informed decision-making.1 Published research highlights numerous applications where accurate risk prediction using EHR data is critical. These include the prediction of cardiovascular disease (CVD) 2, early detection of Alzheimer's Disease and Related Dementias (ADRD) 3, forecasting late-onset preeclampsia 9, and identifying oncology patients at high risk for acute care utilization (ACU).10

### **1.2. Documented Challenges in Clinical Risk Prediction using EHR Data**

Despite the immense potential of EHR data for clinical risk prediction, its utilization is fraught with significant challenges, as extensively documented in the literature. These challenges span data quality, inherent biases, and issues related to model generalizability and calibration.

Data Quality and Standardization:  
A primary challenge stems from the nature of EHR data itself. Unlike data meticulously collected for research purposes, EHR data are primarily generated during routine clinical care.7 Consequently, they often suffer from issues such as a higher incidence of missing values, inconsistencies in data entry, and various potential biases.7 Furthermore, significant challenges persist regarding the standardization of data across different healthcare systems and even within the same system over time, compounded by geographic variability in coding and documentation practices.2 This lack of standardization can impede the development of broadly applicable models and make it difficult to replicate findings or deploy models across different institutions.1 The inherent complexity and heterogeneity of EHR data necessitate the use of sophisticated computational methods to extract meaningful patterns for risk prediction.  
**2\. Bias in EHR Data:**  
EHR data are susceptible to various forms of bias that can compromise the validity and fairness of prediction models.

* **Retrospective Analysis Bias:** The majority of studies using EHR data are retrospective in nature. This design can introduce information bias, particularly related to the post-hoc definition and adjustment of features.7 Moreover, accurately capturing the temporal sequence of clinical events, which is crucial for developing and validating dynamic prediction models, is often limited in retrospective analyses.7 Misinterpretation of these temporal relationships between risk factors and outcomes can lead to the development of flawed models.7  
* **Selection Bias:** EHR databases may not fully reflect broader population trends because they tend to overrepresent individuals who interact more frequently with the healthcare system.7 These individuals may have a higher burden of disease, leading to models that overestimate risk when applied to the general population and potentially resulting in unnecessary interventions or patient anxiety.7  
* **Irregular Patient Visits and Follow-up:** Unlike controlled research studies with predetermined follow-up schedules, EHR data reflect real-world healthcare utilization patterns. Patients have varying frequencies of visits, different intervals between encounters, and variable lengths of follow-up.7 This irregularity complicates consistent data collection and temporal analysis, potentially introducing bias. Such patterns may also reflect underlying differences in disease severity, access to care, or patient health behaviors, further confounding risk prediction efforts.7

**Model Generalizability and Calibration:**  
Risk models developed using data from specific epidemiological cohorts or clinical trials may exhibit limited external generalizability when applied to broader patient populations encountered in routine clinical practice.1 Even models developed within one EHR system may not perform comparably in another due to differences in patient demographics, data capture processes, and local clinical practices.1 For instance, Kolek et al. reported that the CHARGE-AF score, while having good discrimination in its development cohorts, demonstrated poor calibration when applied to their institution's EHR data, tending to underpredict risk in low-risk individuals and overpredict risk in high-risk individuals.1 This underscores a critical need, emphasized in multiple studies, for continuous validation and recalibration of prediction models to ensure their reliability and applicability across diverse populations and evolving healthcare landscapes.2  
**Phenotyping Challenges:**  
A significant hurdle in effectively utilizing EHR data is the lack of precise disease phenotyping.11 While International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes are commonly used, they often fail to capture the detailed clinical nuances, severity, or specific characteristics of a patient's condition.11 This imprecision can affect the accuracy of features derived for risk prediction models.

The confluence of these challenges illustrates a "data rich, information poor" paradox in the context of EHRs. While these systems contain an immense volume of patient data 1, its inherent complexities—related to quality, standardization, bias, and the prevalence of unstructured information 2—mean that this abundance of data does not readily translate into actionable clinical insights for risk prediction using conventional methodologies. This gap between data availability and information utility serves as a strong impetus for the exploration of more advanced artificial intelligence techniques, such as Large Language Models (LLMs), which are hypothesized to be better equipped to navigate these multifaceted complexities.

### **1.3. Overview of Traditional Machine Learning (ML) Approaches with Structured EHR Data**

A variety of traditional machine learning (ML) models have been commonly employed for clinical risk prediction tasks using structured EHR data. These models typically leverage codified information such as patient demographics, diagnostic codes (e.g., ICD-9, ICD-10), medication prescriptions, laboratory test results, and procedure codes as input features.6

Prominent traditional ML approaches reported in the literature include:

* **Logistic Regression (LR):** This statistical model is widely used, often serving as a baseline for comparison due to its simplicity and interpretability.3 LR is particularly common for binary classification problems, where it predicts the probability of an event occurring, such as the development of a specific condition.9  
* **Support Vector Machines (SVM):** SVMs are another established ML technique utilized for classification tasks in clinical prediction.9 For example, Peng et al. (2025) compared the performance of SVMs with LR and XGBoost for predicting late-onset preeclampsia.9  
* **Random Forest (RF):** As an ensemble learning method based on decision trees, RF often demonstrates robust performance and is frequently applied in EHR-based risk prediction.3 A systematic review by Dwibedi et al. (2024) noted that RF models showed superior performance in CVD risk prediction based on pooled AUCs.8  
* **Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM):** This category includes powerful ensemble techniques like XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost, which are frequently reported to achieve high predictive accuracy in clinical settings.3 For instance, Liu et al. (2022) reported that XGBoost demonstrated the best overall performance in predicting 90-day acute care use in oncology patients 10, and Di Vece et al. (2025) found CatBoost achieved good performance for Length of Stay (LoS) prediction.14  
* **Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost):** This is another boosting algorithm mentioned in studies such as the work by Zhou et al. (2025) for early ADRD prediction.3  
* **Traditional Deep Learning (DL) Architectures:** Beyond the more recent LLM architectures, earlier deep learning models such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) applied to sequential or structured data, and graph-based models like the Graph-based Attention Model (GRAM) 6 are also considered traditional in this evolving landscape.6 Dwibedi et al. (2024) also reported high pooled AUCs for deep learning models in CVD risk prediction.8

These models have formed the backbone of EHR-based predictive analytics for many years, demonstrating considerable success in various clinical applications.

### **1.4. Reported Limitations of Traditional ML Methods that LLMs Might Address**

While traditional ML models have achieved notable successes, they also possess inherent limitations, particularly when dealing with the complexities of EHR data. These limitations have spurred research into more advanced techniques like LLMs, which are believed to offer potential solutions.

**Capturing Complex Non-Linear Relationships and Temporal Dynamics:**  
Traditional statistical models often rely on strong assumptions about the underlying data generating process, whereas many ML methods employ more flexible assumptions.18 This flexibility allows ML models to capture complex, non-linear relationships within the data. However, simpler ML models like logistic regression and even standard random forests may not adequately capture the intricate longitudinal and temporal aspects inherent in EHRs.6 The sequence and timing of clinical events are often crucial for accurate risk prediction, and modeling these dynamic patterns effectively can be challenging for methods not explicitly designed for sequential data. LLMs, particularly those based on the transformer architecture, are inherently built to process sequential information and capture long-range dependencies, which could prove advantageous for modeling patient trajectories derived from EHRs.16  
Utilizing Unstructured Data:  
A substantial portion of clinically rich information in EHRs is contained within unstructured narrative text, such as clinical notes, discharge summaries, and radiology reports. Estimates suggest that up to 80% of healthcare documentation is unstructured.12 Traditional ML models primarily operate on structured, coded data and often struggle to incorporate this wealth of unstructured information directly or effectively.12 LLMs, with their advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP) capabilities, excel at processing, understanding, and extracting insights from unstructured text.4 This ability could unlock valuable predictive signals that are currently underutilized, potentially leading to more comprehensive and accurate risk models.  
**Feature Engineering Burden:**  
Developing effective prediction models with traditional ML techniques often involves a significant feature engineering effort.13 This process, which includes selecting relevant medical codes, creating interaction terms, handling temporal aspects, and transforming variables, is typically time-consuming, requires substantial domain expertise, and can become a critical bottleneck in the model development pipeline.13 LLMs, particularly when pre-trained on vast datasets, have the capacity to learn relevant feature representations automatically from raw or minimally processed data.25 LLM-derived embeddings, for example, can serve as rich, context-aware features, potentially alleviating the manual burden associated with traditional feature engineering.  
**Handling High Dimensionality and Sparsity:**  
EHR data are characterized by high dimensionality (a vast number of possible medical codes, lab tests, medications, etc.) and sparsity (any individual patient typically has only a small fraction of these features recorded).37 These characteristics can pose significant challenges for traditional ML algorithms. LLMs and their associated embedding techniques may offer more robust ways to represent such data by capturing underlying semantic relationships between features, even if those features do not frequently co-occur in the training data.35  
**Data Scarcity for Specific Tasks:**  
The performance of standard ML pipelines, especially deep learning methods, can be constrained by the limited availability of task-specific labeled EHR data.6 Deep learning models, in particular, "typically demand substantial data to acquire effective representations".6 LLMs, due to their extensive pre-training on large, diverse datasets, have demonstrated strong performance in low-data or few-shot learning scenarios.25 This capability is particularly valuable in healthcare, where obtaining large labeled datasets for specific or rare outcomes can be difficult and costly. For example, generative LLMs have been reported to show strong performance in predicting COVID-19 severity even in low-data settings.25  
Integrating Supplementary Domain Knowledge:  
Traditional ML pipelines may not inherently or easily incorporate supplementary domain knowledge, such as information from medical textbooks, clinical guidelines, or published research.6 LLMs, having been pre-trained on vast textual corpora that often include a wealth of medical literature, inherently possess extensive embedded domain knowledge.4 This pre-existing knowledge can be leveraged to enhance understanding of clinical data and improve the accuracy of predictive models.  
The limitations of traditional ML models in fully harnessing the complex and often unstructured nature of EHR data suggest that these methods might be approaching a performance plateau for certain clinical prediction tasks. This implicitly positions LLMs as a potential avenue to transcend these limitations, particularly by leveraging their strengths in NLP, representation learning from complex data, and their ability to incorporate vast pre-trained knowledge. However, the choice of any predictive model, traditional or LLM-based, must also consider the specific clinical context and intended use. As highlighted by Wijeysundera et al. (2017), factors beyond raw predictive power, such as model calibration and the clinical implications of different types of errors, are critical.1 For example, a model with excellent discrimination (e.g., high AUROC) might still be unsuitable if it is poorly calibrated and the clinical application requires accurate absolute risk estimates for decision-making.1 This nuanced perspective is important when evaluating whether newer, more complex models like LLMs offer a tangible advantage over simpler, potentially better-understood traditional models for specific clinical scenarios.

## **Section 2: Large Language Models in Healthcare: Potential and Early Applications**

The rapid advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have generated considerable interest across various domains, with healthcare being a particularly promising area for their application. This section provides a brief review of the evolution and general capabilities of LLMs, summarizes published literature on their emerging applications in the medical domain, and discusses early attempts and findings regarding their use for clinical predictive tasks using EHR data.

### **2.1. Brief Review of the Evolution and General Capabilities of LLMs**

The development of LLMs represents a significant trajectory in the field of artificial intelligence and natural language processing (NLP). The journey began with early NLP systems and rule-based expert systems in the 1960s through the 1980s.29 This was followed by the rise of statistical NLP methods in the 2000s, which improved the interpretation of electronic text.29 A notable milestone was the introduction of Word2Vec in 2013, which provided efficient methods for learning word embeddings from raw text, capturing semantic relationships between words.29

The true revolution in LLM development, however, was catalyzed by the advent of the Transformer architecture, introduced by Google in 2017 with the paper "Attention is All You Need".26 This architecture, with its self-attention mechanism, allowed models to weigh the importance of different words in a sequence and capture long-range dependencies more effectively than preceding architectures like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.42 Key LLMs that mark this evolution include BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) and GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer), both emerging around 2018\.29 Subsequent iterations, such as GPT-3 (175 billion parameters, released in 2020), ChatGPT (introduced in 2022), and GPT-4 (released in 2023), have demonstrated progressively sophisticated capabilities due to increased model scale (both in terms of parameter count and the volume of training data) and architectural refinements.26 For instance, domain-specific models like GatorTron-large have also scaled significantly, reaching 8.9 billion parameters.45 This rapid evolution from general NLP successes to exploration within the medical domain indicates a strong perceived applicability and an urgent need for advanced NLP capabilities in healthcare settings.

The general capabilities of LLMs are extensive. At their core, LLMs are designed to comprehend, generate, and interact through natural language with remarkable fluency.26 They excel at a wide array of NLP tasks, including but not limited to text classification, named entity recognition, sentiment analysis, text summarization, question answering, and machine translation.26 These models leverage deep learning algorithms and are typically pre-trained on vast and diverse text corpora, enabling them to learn intricate grammatical structures, lexical nuances, and semantic contexts.26 An important trend in LLM development is the move towards "Foundation Models." These are large-scale models pre-trained on broad data that can be adapted to a wide range of downstream tasks with minimal or no task-specific fine-tuning.48 LLMs are a prominent example of such foundation models. Furthermore, modern LLMs are increasingly capable of processing multimodal data, integrating information from text, images, and audio, which is particularly relevant for the complex data landscape of healthcare.25

### **2.2. Summarize Published Literature on Emerging Applications of LLMs in the Medical Domain**

The versatile capabilities of LLMs have led to their exploration in numerous medical applications, moving beyond their initial text-centric origins towards more integrated and multimodal functionalities.

* **Analysis of Clinical Notes and Summarization:** LLMs are being extensively investigated for their ability to analyze and summarize unstructured clinical notes, including physician progress notes, patient histories, and discharge summaries.4 The aim is often to reduce documentation burden for clinicians, extract key information efficiently, and help identify trends within patient data.28 Some studies report that LLM-generated summaries of clinical texts can be comparable or even superior to those produced by medical experts in terms of completeness, correctness, and conciseness, with models like ChatGPT being favored for readability in medical dialogue summarization.55 Xu et al. (2024) (referenced in 56) have also developed benchmarks like MedGPTEval to evaluate LLM responses in tasks such as summarization.  
* **Medical Question Answering (MQA):** LLMs have demonstrated significant promise in answering medical questions posed by both clinicians and patients.26 Specialized models such as Med-PaLM 2 have achieved high accuracy on medical licensing examinations like the USMLE, reportedly surpassing "passing" scores and outperforming their predecessors.29 In some evaluations, physicians have indicated a preference for Med-PaLM 2 answers over those from other physicians across several clinical axes.57 The ability of LLMs to query and summarize unstructured medical notes, for example in intensive care settings, has also been a focus of research.26  
* **Support for Diagnostic Reasoning:** LLMs are being explored as tools to assist healthcare professionals in the diagnostic process, potentially enhancing accuracy and speed.26 A notable study by Qazi et al. (2025) found that physicians who were trained to use an LLM (GPT-4o) and had access to it during diagnostic tasks significantly outperformed their peers who relied only on conventional resources, achieving mean diagnostic reasoning scores of 71.4% versus 42.6%.63 Interestingly, the LLM alone performed even better than the physician group using the LLM.63 Furthermore, LLMs have reportedly shown an ability to surpass average human consensus in diagnosing rare and complex medical cases.47  
* **Other Emerging Applications:** The application of LLMs in medicine extends to several other areas:  
  * **Patient Information Generation and Education:** LLMs are used to generate educational materials for patients, simplify complex medical texts like radiology reports into patient-friendly language, and translate medical information.55  
  * **Healthcare Administration:** Automation of administrative tasks is a significant area of interest. This includes summarizing clinical notes, extracting structured data from pathology reports, generating various types of reports, and assisting with medical billing code assignment.29  
  * **Drug Discovery and Medical Research:** LLMs are contributing to the early stages of drug discovery by analyzing complex chemical and biological data, predicting drug interactions, and facilitating the screening of new compounds.26 They also assist in medical research by helping with literature reviews and academic writing.47  
  * **Personalized Medicine:** As part of broader foundation model initiatives, LLMs are envisioned to play a role in tailoring medical treatments to individual patients by integrating diverse data sources, including genomic and lifestyle data.26

This expansion from purely text-based applications to more integrated, multimodal systems reflects the evolving understanding of how LLMs and foundation models can address the multifaceted nature of healthcare data and clinical decision-making. The initial focus on text processing was a natural starting point, given LLMs' core strengths, but the recognition that clinical reality involves diverse data types (structured data, images, notes) is driving the field towards models that can holistically interpret this information.52

### **2.3. Discuss Early Attempts and Findings Reported in the Literature Regarding the Use of LLMs for Clinical Predictive Tasks using EHR Data**

The application of LLMs to clinical predictive tasks using EHR data is an active area of research, with early studies exploring various methodologies and yielding a range of findings. A central theme in these early attempts is the challenge of adapting models primarily designed for natural language text to the often structured, coded, and heterogeneous nature of EHR data.

Researchers have investigated converting structured EHR data—such as diagnoses, laboratory results, and prescriptions—into natural language narratives. This "textualization" or "serialization" allows LLMs to process this information and potentially leverage their pre-trained medical knowledge for predictive modeling.39 Studies frequently evaluate the zero-shot (no examples provided in the prompt) and few-shot (a small number of examples provided) performance of LLMs for EHR-based disease prediction, often employing various prompt engineering strategies to guide the models.11

A novel approach in this domain involves the use of **LLM agents**. For example, Wang et al. (2025) proposed the EHR-CoAgent framework, which utilizes two LLM agents: a predictor agent that makes predictions and generates reasoning, and a critic agent that analyzes incorrect predictions by the predictor.39 The critic then provides feedback to refine the predictor's prompts, enabling the system to learn from its mistakes and improve its performance on EHR-based disease prediction tasks. This approach reportedly achieved decent few-shot performance when compared to traditional supervised learning methods.39

A significant portion of early research has focused on **domain-specific LLMs**, which are pre-trained or fine-tuned on biomedical and clinical corpora. Notable examples include:

* **BioBERT, ClinicalBERT, and Med-BERT:** These models adapt the BERT architecture by training on large biomedical texts (e.g., PubMed abstracts for BioBERT 45) or clinical data (e.g., MIMIC-III clinical notes for ClinicalBERT 45, and a massive dataset of 28.49 million patient EHRs for Med-BERT 81). Rasmy et al. (2021) reported that Med-BERT substantially improved accuracy in disease prediction tasks, particularly when fine-tuning datasets were small.81 ClinicalBERT has been fine-tuned for tasks such as predicting 30-day hospital readmission and in-hospital mortality, often outperforming baseline models.22  
* **GatorTron:** This is a large clinical LLM, with versions scaling up to 8.9 billion parameters, trained on a combination of UF Health clinical notes, PubMed articles, and Wikipedia.45 It has shown improved performance on NLP tasks like Natural Language Inference (NLI) and Medical Question Answering (MQA).45

Concurrently, studies have also explored the use of **general-purpose LLMs** (e.g., models from the GPT series) for clinical prediction. One approach involves feeding serialized EHR data directly to these models for prediction. However, findings here have been mixed. Brown et al. (2024) found that traditional ML models (gradient-boosting trees) outperformed non-fine-tuned GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 in terms of AUROC and calibration for clinical prediction tasks using EHR data, although they noted that LLM performance is improving with newer versions.85 In contrast, Arsanjani et al. (2025) demonstrated that when **general-purpose LLMs are used to *encode* serialized EHR data into embeddings, these embeddings can match or even surpass the performance of specialized EHR foundation models like CLMBR-T-Base on certain tasks.**40 This suggests that the method of application—direct prediction versus feature extraction—is a critical factor.

Early LLM-based predictive models have been applied to a range of clinical outcomes, including:

* Mortality prediction 78  
* Hospital readmission risk 22  
* Prediction of disease onset or specific diagnoses (e.g., dementia, seizures, next disease prediction) 6  
* Prediction of ICU bouncebacks and length of stay 4

Despite promising results, early applications have also highlighted several **challenges**:

* **Context Window Limitations:** LLMs have a finite input length (context window), and lengthy clinical notes or extensive patient histories, even when serialized, can exceed this limit, potentially leading to the loss of important information.11 Researchers are exploring preprocessing frameworks to filter and highlight key information from long texts to mitigate this.11  
* **Reliability and Factual Accuracy:** Ensuring the empirical reliability of LLM outputs and addressing issues like "hallucinations" (generating plausible but incorrect information) remain significant concerns, especially in the high-stakes medical domain.26 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which grounds LLM responses in external knowledge sources, is one strategy being investigated to improve factual accuracy.33  
* **Ethical Considerations:** Data privacy, algorithmic bias, and accountability are critical ethical considerations that need careful management when deploying LLMs in healthcare.9

The early findings in using LLMs for clinical prediction from EHR data reveal a nuanced landscape. Domain-specific LLMs, pre-trained or extensively fine-tuned on relevant medical data like Med-BERT, often demonstrate clear advantages, particularly in scenarios with limited task-specific labeled data.81 This suggests the value of specialized training for capturing the unique patterns within EHR data. Conversely, the application of general-purpose LLMs (such as GPT-3.5 or GPT-4) directly for predictive tasks on structured EHR data has, in some studies, yielded performance that is not superior to, and sometimes inferior to, traditional machine learning approaches, especially concerning aspects like model calibration.85 However, when these general-purpose LLMs are employed as powerful encoders to generate embeddings from carefully textualized EHR data, these embeddings can prove competitive with, or even outperform, those from specialized EHR foundation models.40 This dichotomy underscores that the method of application is paramount; "off-the-shelf" use of general LLMs for direct prediction with simply serialized EHRs may be less effective than leveraging them as sophisticated feature extractors or undertaking substantial fine-tuning on domain-specific EHR datasets.

## **Section 3: Leveraging LLM-derived Embeddings for Structured EHR Data Analysis**

The transformation of complex, often sparse, structured Electronic Health Record (EHR) data into meaningful representations for predictive modeling is a significant challenge. Large Language Models (LLMs) offer a novel approach through the generation of embeddings, which can serve as rich feature representations. This section delves into the concept of LLM-derived embeddings, the challenges of applying text-focused LLMs to structured EHR data, methods for serializing this data into LLM-compatible formats, and a comparative discussion of the performance of these embeddings against traditional machine learning techniques and between different types of LLMs.

### **3.1. Explanation of Embeddings as Feature Representations from LLMs**

LLM embeddings are dense vector representations of input data—be it words, phrases, entire texts, or, in the context of multimodal models, other data types like images or codes.35 These embeddings are a core output of LLMs, capturing the semantic meaning, contextual nuances, and intricate relationships between different data tokens or concepts learned during the model's extensive pre-training phase on massive datasets.35 Unlike traditional sparse representations such as one-hot encoding, which often result in very high-dimensional vectors with limited explicit structure, LLM embeddings are dense, meaning most elements in the vector are non-zero, allowing them to capture a wealth of information more efficiently.35 The self-attention mechanisms inherent in transformer architectures, which underpin most modern LLMs, play a crucial role in generating these contextually rich embeddings by dynamically weighing the importance of different input tokens relative to each other.36

The potential benefits of using LLM-derived embeddings for clinical feature representation are manifold:

* **Contextual Awareness:** A key advantage of LLM embeddings over traditional word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) is their ability to account for the context in which a term or code appears.35 In the medical domain, where terminology can be highly nuanced and context-dependent, this capability is vital for accurate representation.  
* **Semantic Similarity:** LLM embeddings can map semantically similar medical concepts or patient states to points that are close to each other in the vector space, even if these concepts are described using different terminologies or codes.35 For instance, the GRASP methodology demonstrated that concepts like "High glucose level in blood" and "Hyperglycemia" could be embedded closely due to their semantic equivalence, facilitating better generalization.38 This ability to capture semantic relationships is particularly powerful for handling the diversity of medical coding systems and natural language expressions found in EHRs.  
* **Transferability and Few-Shot Learning:** Embeddings pre-trained on large, general, or domain-specific corpora can capture broad linguistic and semantic patterns. These pre-trained representations can then be transferred and fine-tuned for specific downstream clinical prediction tasks, often improving performance, especially in scenarios where task-specific labeled data is scarce.35  
* **Automated Feature Engineering:** LLMs can learn these complex representations automatically from data, thereby reducing the substantial manual effort and domain expertise typically required for traditional feature engineering in EHR-based modeling.11 The embeddings themselves become the features fed into downstream predictive models.

The capacity of LLM embeddings to capture semantic richness beyond what is apparent from raw medical codes offers a significant advancement. Traditional methods often treat medical codes as discrete, independent identifiers (e.g., through one-hot encoding). This means such models do not inherently understand, for example, that "Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus without complications" is closely related to "Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus with neuropathy." LLMs, particularly those exposed to medical literature or extensive clinical text during pre-training, can learn these semantic connections. Consequently, their embeddings can place related concepts near each other in the vector space.35 This allows a downstream model to potentially generalize better; for instance, if it has learned that "Code A" is predictive of an outcome, it might infer similar predictiveness for "Code B" if Code B is semantically very similar to Code A, even if Code B was rare in the training data. This ability to infer relationships based on learned semantics, rather than solely on observed co-occurrences, is a key advantage over methods that do not capture such underlying meaning.

### **3.2. Challenges in Applying LLMs (Designed for Text) to Structured/Tabular EHR Data**

Despite the promise of LLM embeddings, applying models primarily designed for natural language text to the structured and tabular nature of much EHR data presents several challenges:

* **Data Format Mismatch:** LLMs are fundamentally architected to process sequences of text. Structured EHR data, however, typically consists of tables containing numerical values (e.g., lab results, vital signs), categorical codes (e.g., ICD for diagnoses, RxNorm for medications, LOINC for labs, CPT for procedures), and demographic information.17 Converting this structured information into a linear textual format that an LLM can effectively process is a non-trivial task.  
* **High Dimensionality and Sparsity:** The space of possible medical codes and features in EHRs is vast, leading to very high-dimensional data. However, for any individual patient, only a small fraction of these codes will be present, resulting in highly sparse data matrices.37 Representing this sparsity and high dimensionality in a textual sequence that an LLM can meaningfully interpret without losing information or introducing noise is a significant hurdle.  
* **Hidden Contextual Dependencies:** Structured EHR data often contains implicit contextual dependencies between various medical features (e.g., a particular diagnosis might imply certain medications or lab abnormalities) that are not always explicitly stated in a simple tabular layout.37 LLMs need to be able to infer these dependencies from the serialized input.  
* **Numerical Representation:** The tokenization and representation of numerical values (like laboratory results or age) by LLMs can be problematic. Some models may split numbers into individual digit tokens or lack a coherent way to represent decimal values, potentially losing the quantitative meaning crucial for clinical assessment.101  
* **Order and Sequence:** While patient medical histories inherently possess a temporal sequence (visits over time), the optimal way to order features within a single time point or across different types of structured data (e.g., demographics, then labs, then diagnoses) for LLM input is not always clear. Some tabular data might be order-invariant, and imposing an artificial sequence could affect LLM interpretation.101  
* **Limited Context Window:** LLMs have a finite context window, meaning they can only process a limited amount of input text at a time. Long patient histories, even when serialized into text, can easily exceed these limits, necessitating truncation or summarization strategies that might lead to the loss of important historical information.11  
* **Lack of Domain-Specific Pre-training for Tabular Modality:** Most general-purpose LLMs are pre-trained on massive corpora of natural language text, not specifically on the unique "language" or structure of serialized tabular medical codes or EHR data sequences.77 This means they may lack innate understanding of the conventions and patterns within this specific data modality without further adaptation or very effective serialization.

### **3.3. Survey of Published Methods for Serializing or Converting Tabular EHR Data into Textual Formats**

To bridge the gap between structured EHR data and text-native LLMs, researchers have explored various methods for serializing or converting tabular data into textual formats. The overarching goal of these serialization techniques is to transform structured information—such as medical codes, laboratory values, and demographic details—into a sequential textual representation that LLMs can process, while ideally preserving the semantic meaning and relational context of the original data. The choice and quality of the serialization method are critical, as it forms the input to the LLM and directly influences the utility of any derived embeddings or predictions. Poor serialization can lead to information loss or the introduction of artifacts that hinder the LLM's comprehension, whereas effective serialization can make complex structured data more amenable to LLM analysis.

Several approaches to EHR data serialization have been documented in the literature:

* **Template-Based Serialization:** This method involves using predefined templates to structure the tabular data into a textual narrative. A common template might be of the form "{attribute name} is {value}" for each feature-value pair in the EHR.77 For example, a diagnosis code might be serialized as "Patient has a diagnosis of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus." Arsanjani et al. (2025) adopted a strategy of converting structured EHR data into markdown-formatted plain text documents, where medical codes are replaced with their natural language descriptions.40 Similarly, Hong et al. (2024) proposed transforming tabular data into a textual format to train LLMs, subsequently using the LLM to generate new synthetic text data for augmentation, which is then converted back into a tabular format.103  
* **Structured Separation using Special Characters:** To maintain some semblance of the original tabular structure within a linear text sequence, special characters or delimiters can be used to separate different data elements, such as columns, rows, or individual data points.102 For instance, the SoLM (Structured-Object Language Model) uses specific tokens like \<SEP\> (separator) and \<END\> (end of value) to delineate keys and values in a sequence of key-value pairs derived from structured data.104  
* **Generative Descriptions or Self-Derived Summaries:** In this approach, an LLM itself is employed to generate a textual summary or description of the EHR data.102 The study by Agbavor and Liang (referenced in 102) indicated that using such self-generated serializations, particularly when incorporating all EHR features, led to notable improvements in performance. This method essentially uses an LLM to perform an initial layer of interpretation and feature abstraction.  
* **Converting Codes to Natural Language Descriptions:** A widely adopted technique involves mapping standardized medical codes (e.g., ICD for diagnoses, LOINC for lab tests, RxNorm for medications) to their official textual descriptions or more human-readable names.38 This leverages the LLM's inherent understanding of natural language and the medical terminology present in its pre-training corpus. The GRASP methodology, for example, uses the natural-language names of OMOP (Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership) concepts as input to the embedding LLM.38  
* **Event Sequence Serialization:** This approach treats a patient's EHR history as a chronological sequence of clinical events (e.g., diagnoses, procedures, medication administrations), analogous to words forming a sentence.19 This is a common input format for BERT-based models adapted for EHR data, such as Med-BERT and BEHRT. Makarov & Lipkovich (2025) proposed an encoder-decoder architecture for next disease prediction that uses a patient's past disease history serialized as a sequence.91  
* **Symbolic Time Series Approximation (STSA):** For time-series data within EHRs (e.g., continuous vital signs), methods like LLM-ABBA employ STSA to convert numerical time series into sequences of symbols.106 This discretizes the continuous data into a format more akin to text tokens, making it digestible for LLMs.  
* **Feature-Selective and First-Occurrence Sequence Serialization:** As investigated by Li et al. (2025) in the context of EHR audit log data, this involves creating textual sequences by either selecting a subset of the most important features (top-k) or by only including the first occurrence of each feature within a given timeframe.105 These methods aim to reduce the input length and complexity while retaining salient information.  
* **Combination with Unstructured Notes:** Some methodologies naturally lend themselves to integrating serialized structured data with existing unstructured clinical notes from the EHR. The combined textual input can then be fed to an LLM, providing a richer, multimodal (text \+ structured-turned-text) representation of the patient.80

The choice of serialization strategy can significantly affect the performance of downstream tasks, as it dictates how well the LLM can interpret the inherent structure and meaning of the original EHR data.

### **3.4. Discussion of Studies Comparing Performance of LLM-derived Features from Simple Serializations against Traditional ML Models using Raw Numerical/Categorical Data**

The comparative performance of LLM-derived features from serialized EHR data versus traditional ML models operating on raw or conventionally processed structured EHR data is a subject of ongoing investigation, with current literature presenting a mixed picture.

Several studies suggest that direct application of general-purpose LLMs to simply serialized EHR data for predictive tasks may not consistently outperform well-established traditional ML models. Brown et al. (2024) conducted a notable comparison where traditional ML models, specifically gradient-boosting trees, demonstrated substantially better performance than non-fine-tuned GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 for clinical prediction tasks (predicting discharge within 24 hours) using EHR data from VUMC and MIMIC-IV ED datasets.85 In this study, traditional ML achieved significantly higher AUROC scores (e.g., 0.847 for ML vs. 0.629 for GPT-4 on VUMC data) and showed markedly better model calibration, as measured by the Brier Score.85 This suggests that for direct prediction using relatively simple serialization strategies, current general-purpose LLMs might not yet match the efficacy of specialized ML algorithms that are adept at handling structured numerical and categorical inputs. Similarly, a study by Li et al. (2025) on predicting user errors from EHR audit logs found that a LightGBM model (a traditional ML technique) outperformed fine-tuned LLMs (LLaMA-2 7B and DeBERTa-v3-small) that processed various serialized versions of the log sequences.105 For example, the LGBM achieved an AUC of 0.86, while the best-performing LLaMA-2 configuration reached an AUC of 0.81.105

However, the narrative changes when LLMs are used not as direct predictors with simple serializations, but as sophisticated feature extractors or embedders. Arsanjani et al. (2025) explored the use of general-purpose LLMs to encode structured EHR data (serialized into markdown-formatted plain text with medical codes converted to natural language descriptions) into high-dimensional embeddings.40 Their findings indicated that these LLM-derived embeddings could often match or even surpass the performance of a specialized EHR foundation model (CLMBR-T-Base) across a diverse set of 15 clinical prediction tasks from the EHRSHOT benchmark.40 This suggests that the utility of LLMs might be more pronounced when they are leveraged for their representation learning capabilities to generate rich features from textualized EHR data, which are then used by simpler downstream classifiers.

The GRASP methodology further supports the potential of LLM-derived semantic features.38 This approach uses LLM embeddings of medical concept names (derived from a general-purpose model, OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-large) as input to a downstream transformer network. In internal validation, GRASP’s performance was comparable to XGBoost, and it demonstrated superior transferability to external datasets compared to XGBoost.38 This highlights the benefit of semantic understanding captured by LLM embeddings, especially for model generalization.

Liu et al. (referenced in 77) also touched upon this by noting that for clinical tabular data, a straightforward manual serialization template (e.g., "The {attribute} is {value}.") could yield competitive results, implying that LLMs might be more reliant on the correct values being present in the serialized text than on a highly complex structural representation of the features.77

In summary, the current body of research indicates that simply serializing structured EHR data and using general-purpose LLMs for direct zero-shot or few-shot prediction does not guarantee performance superior to traditional ML models optimized for tabular data. In fact, traditional ML often excels in such direct comparisons, particularly in terms of calibration. However, when LLMs are employed to generate embeddings or features from more thoughtfully textualized EHR data, these representations can be highly effective and may offer advantages in terms of semantic richness, generalization, and performance on par with or exceeding specialized models, especially in few-shot settings or when model transferability is crucial. The specific methodology of how the LLM is integrated into the predictive pipeline (e.g., as an end-to-end predictor versus a feature embedder) and the sophistication of the data serialization appear to be key determinants of success.

### **3.5. Summarize Literature Comparing Effectiveness of Embeddings from General-Purpose LLMs versus those from LLMs with Specialized Medical Knowledge or Pre-training on Medical Corpora**

The comparative effectiveness of embeddings derived from general-purpose LLMs versus those from medically specialized LLMs (i.e., models pre-trained or fine-tuned on medical corpora) for clinical prediction tasks is an area of active investigation, with studies presenting nuanced findings.

Arguments and Evidence for Specialized LLM Embeddings:  
A significant body of work suggests that LLMs specifically adapted to the medical domain through pre-training or fine-tuning on relevant corpora (such as biomedical literature or clinical EHR data) tend to produce embeddings that are more effective for specialized clinical tasks.

* Rasmy et al. (2021) demonstrated with Med-BERT, which was pre-trained on a large cohort of structured EHR data, that the resulting contextualized embeddings substantially boosted AUC scores in disease prediction tasks, particularly when the fine-tuning datasets were small, compared to models without such specialized pre-training.47 This implies that embeddings from medically specialized LLMs can better capture clinically relevant patterns from EHR data.  
* Similarly, ClinicalBERT, which was further pre-trained on clinical notes from MIMIC-III, showed superior performance over general BERT on various clinical NLP tasks and in predicting hospital readmission.22 This suggests that fine-tuning on in-domain clinical text enhances the model's ability to generate useful representations for clinical applications.  
* A study by Wang et al. (2025) directly compared embeddings from different LLMs for detecting influenza cases from EHR data.100 They found that domain-specific LLMs, such as Med-BERT, consistently outperformed general-purpose models (like the original BERT and OpenAI embeddings) in both local modeling and direct transfer learning scenarios. While generic OpenAI embeddings could achieve good performance, they often required fine-tuning to reach their optimal potential in the clinical context.100  
* In a named entity recognition (NER) task for cancer symptoms (anxiety, nausea/vomiting), Symptom-BERT (which involved further pre-training Bio-Clinical-BERT) achieved higher F1-scores than Symptom-GPT (a GPT-2 model further pre-trained), particularly for physical symptoms.109 This again points to the benefits of domain-specific adaptation.

Arguments and Evidence for General-Purpose LLM Embeddings:  
Conversely, other research indicates that large general-purpose LLMs can generate highly competitive, and sometimes superior, embeddings for clinical tasks, especially when EHR data is effectively textualized or when broader semantic understanding is required.

* Arsanjani et al. (2025) reported that embeddings derived from general-purpose LLMs (specifically GTE-Qwen2-7B and LLM2Vec-Llama3.1-8B) applied to serialized EHR data (where codes were replaced by natural language descriptions) could often match or even outperform embeddings from a specialized EHR foundation model (CLMBR-T-Base) across 15 diverse clinical prediction tasks from the EHRSHOT benchmark.40 The effectiveness of these general-purpose embeddings was also observed to scale with the size of the underlying LLM and the available context window.  
* A study focusing on semantic search within clinical documents found that generalist embedding models (e.g., BGE) actually performed *better* than clinical-specialized models at matching rephrased clinical text snippets to their original descriptions.110 The authors hypothesized that some existing clinical specialized models might be overly sensitive to minor variations in input phrasing due to having been trained on less diverse datasets, thereby lacking the "reliable global language understanding" that characterizes large generalist models.110  
* The GRASP model, which achieved strong performance and transferability in predicting multiple disease outcomes, utilized OpenAI’s "text-embedding-3-large" model—a general-purpose embedding model—to create semantic representations of medical concepts based on their natural language names.38 This successful application suggests that general-purpose models can effectively capture medical semantics when the input is appropriately formulated as natural language.

Nuance, Task-Dependency, and Future Directions:  
**The existing literature does not offer a definitive judgment that one type of LLM embedding is universally superior.** The optimal choice appears to be task-dependent and influenced by the nature of the input data and the specific requirements of the clinical application.96

* For tasks that require a deep understanding of specific clinical coding systems, EHR data structures, or highly specialized medical jargon deeply embedded in clinical notes, embeddings from models specifically pre-trained or fine-tuned on such data (like Med-BERT or ClinicalBERT) may hold an advantage.  
* For tasks that benefit from broader semantic understanding, robustness to variations in language (e.g., rephrasing in notes or concept descriptions), or when dealing with natural language descriptions of medical concepts, embeddings from large general-purpose LLMs might be more effective or require less task-specific adaptation.

This nuanced landscape suggests a potential convergence or a "best of both worlds" approach. The vast pre-training of general-purpose LLMs endows them with a powerful foundation of world knowledge and language understanding. This foundation can then be further honed through domain-adaptive pre-training or fine-tuning on large-scale, diverse medical and EHR datasets. Such a strategy could combine the breadth of general models with the depth of specialized ones, potentially leading to more robust and versatile embeddings for clinical risk prediction. The finding by Wang et al. (2025) that general OpenAI embeddings improve with fine-tuning 100, and the success of Arsanjani et al. (2025) with general LLM embeddings on textualized EHRs 40, point towards the viability of adapting powerful generalist architectures for specific medical applications rather than relying solely on smaller models trained exclusively on limited domain data.

## **Section 4: The Role of Prompt Engineering in Optimizing LLM-derived Clinical Embeddings**

Prompt engineering, the art and science of crafting effective inputs to guide Large Language Models (LLMs), has emerged as a critical factor in harnessing their capabilities. While much of the research in the medical domain has focused on using prompt engineering to refine direct textual outputs such as summaries, answers to questions, or classifications, the principles underlying this practice strongly suggest its potential applicability to enhancing the quality and utility of embeddings derived from LLMs when processing serialized clinical data. This section provides an overview of prompt engineering, reviews common prompting strategies, synthesizes research on how advanced prompting might improve LLM-derived clinical embeddings, and discusses the impact of input representation quality and prompting on the utility of these embeddings.

### **4.1. Overview of Prompt Engineering for LLMs**

Prompt engineering refers to the strategic design and refinement of input queries (prompts) given to LLMs to elicit desired, accurate, and useful responses.70 Unlike traditional programming where explicit commands dictate behavior, interacting with LLMs is more akin to providing instructions or context to a highly knowledgeable but generalist agent. The effectiveness of an LLM is often highly sensitive to the way it is prompted; even minor variations in wording, structure, or the inclusion of examples can significantly alter the quality, relevance, and style of the output.113 The fundamental goal of prompt engineering is to provide sufficient structure and clarity in the input to precisely guide the LLM's internal processing and subsequent generation, without modifying the model's underlying parameters.114

### **4.2. Review of Common Prompting Strategies and Their General Applications**

A variety of prompting strategies have been developed and documented, each with its own characteristics and suitable applications:

* **Zero-Shot Prompting:** In this approach, the LLM is tasked with performing an action or answering a question based solely on its pre-trained knowledge, without any task-specific examples provided within the prompt itself.71 For example, a zero-shot prompt might be, "Classify the sentiment of the following clinical note excerpt: 'Patient reports significant improvement in pain levels.'".118  
* **Few-Shot Prompting:** This technique involves providing the LLM with a small number (typically one to a few) of illustrative examples (demonstrations) of the desired input-output behavior directly within the prompt.39 These examples help the model understand the task, context, and expected format. For instance, one might provide a few serialized EHR snippets paired with their risk classifications before asking the LLM to classify a new snippet.  
* **Persona-Based / Role Prompting:** This strategy involves assigning a specific role or persona to the LLM, such as "You are an experienced cardiologist" or "Act as a clinical data analyst".70 The intention is to guide the LLM's responses to align with the knowledge, style, tone, and focus associated with that persona. Research suggests that using non-intimate interpersonal roles and gender-neutral terms can sometimes yield better results.122  
* **Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting:** CoT prompting encourages the LLM to articulate a series of intermediate reasoning steps before producing a final answer.69 This is often achieved by including phrases like "Let's think step by step" in the prompt or by providing few-shot examples that demonstrate the reasoning process. CoT is particularly beneficial for tasks requiring complex logical deduction or multi-step problem-solving. A specialized variant, the Medical Structured Reasoning (MSR) process, has been proposed to guide LLMs through cognitive steps analogous to clinical diagnostic reasoning.69  
* **Instruction-Based Prompting:** This involves giving the LLM explicit instructions regarding the task, the desired format of the output, the tone to adopt, or specific constraints to adhere to.70  
* **Elaborated Prompting:** This is an extension of basic prompting where the query is refined with additional details, clarifications, or contextual information to help the LLM better understand the user's intent.70  
* **Self-Consistency Prompting:** Often used in conjunction with CoT, this technique involves generating multiple responses or reasoning paths from a stochastic LLM (e.g., by using a non-zero temperature setting) and then selecting the most frequently occurring or consistent answer as the final output.116 This can improve the robustness of answers for complex reasoning tasks.  
* **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** While RAG is a broader architectural pattern, it heavily involves prompt engineering. RAG systems first retrieve relevant information from an external knowledge base (e.g., medical textbooks, research articles, or a curated database of clinical guidelines) based on the input query. This retrieved information is then incorporated into the prompt provided to the LLM, thereby grounding the LLM's response in factual, domain-specific data.11 RAG is considered crucial for mitigating hallucinations and enhancing the accuracy and reliability of LLM outputs in specialized domains like medicine.

### **4.3. Synthesize Existing Research on the Use of Advanced Prompting Techniques to Improve LLM-derived Clinical Embeddings**

Direct published research that explicitly investigates how advanced prompting techniques—such as Persona or Chain-of-Thought—specifically improve the *quality of embeddings* generated by LLMs from serialized clinical EHR data for downstream prediction tasks is still an emerging area. Much of the existing literature on prompt engineering in medicine focuses on optimizing the LLM's direct textual outputs (e.g., for summarization, question answering, or direct classification from text) rather than on the intermediate representations (embeddings) themselves.69

However, the underlying principles of how LLMs process information suggest a strong theoretical basis for the utility of prompt engineering in enhancing embedding quality. LLM embeddings are derived from the model's internal activation states as it processes an input sequence. Therefore, any prompting strategy that guides the LLM to process the input in a more focused, contextually relevant, or semantically deep manner should, in theory, lead to internal states—and thus embeddings—that better capture the desired characteristics for a given task. This can be thought of as prompt engineering acting as a latent optimizer for the embedding generation process, implicitly guiding feature extraction or directing the LLM's attention towards the most salient aspects of the serialized clinical data.

**Hypothesized Benefits of Advanced Prompting for Clinical Embedding Quality:**

* **Improving Quality, Discriminative Power, or Semantic Richness:**  
  * **Persona Prompting:** If an LLM is prompted to adopt the persona of a "clinical informatics expert" or a "differential diagnostician" before processing serialized EHR data, it might be guided to focus on patterns and features most relevant to clinical outcomes. This could lead to embeddings that are more discriminative for specific prediction tasks. As noted in general prompting literature, role prompting can enhance task performance in reasoning and explanation 122, which could translate to more reasoned internal representations.  
  * **Chain-of-Thought (CoT) for Feature Abstraction:** While CoT is typically used to generate explicit reasoning steps in text, an adapted version could be used to prompt the LLM to internally "identify key predictive features" or "evaluate the combined risk from these observations" within the serialized EHR data. Even if these intermediate "thoughts" are not explicitly outputted, the process of generating them could shape the final embedding to better reflect this synthesized understanding. The Medical Structured Reasoning process described by Lyu et al. (2025) for medical Q\&A, which guides LLMs through cognitive steps like recalling relevant knowledge and analyzing information 69, exemplifies how such structured prompting could lead to deeper processing relevant for embedding generation.  
  * **Few-Shot Prompting with Serialized EHR Examples:** Providing the LLM with a few examples of serialized EHR snippets from patients with known clinical outcomes (e.g., examples of high-risk vs. low-risk patient profiles) within the prompt, before it processes and embeds a new patient's serialized record, could prime the model. This priming might align the generated embeddings more closely with the feature space relevant to the downstream prediction task. The significant improvements seen with few-shot prompting in the LLaMA-RAG model for postoperative mortality prediction from clinical notes 78 support the general power of in-context examples.  
* Guiding LLMs to Activate or Leverage Pre-trained Clinical Knowledge:  
  LLMs, especially large general-purpose models, are often pre-trained on vast and diverse corpora, which may include substantial amounts of medical literature, textbooks, and other health-related documents.40 Prompt engineering could be a key mechanism to "unlock" or "activate" this latent clinical knowledge when the LLM encounters serialized EHR data.  
  * Prompts that explicitly instruct the LLM to, for example, "interpret this patient record in the context of established cardiovascular risk factors" or "consider the potential drug-drug interactions implied by this medication list" when processing serialized EHRs, could encourage the model to map the input data to its pre-existing medical knowledge structures.  
  * The work by Dietrich et al. (2025) 120 and discussions by RTS Labs 114 on using prompts to guide LLMs for specific medical tasks implicitly rely on this activation of relevant knowledge. The structured reasoning prompts detailed in 69 explicitly aim to make the LLM recall relevant medical knowledge as part of its process.  
  * Similarly, studies on converting clinical reports to FHIR format using prompt engineering 121 demonstrate that prompts help the LLM apply its understanding to structured, domain-specific tasks, which likely involves activating relevant learned concepts.

If an LLM can be guided to process serialized EHR data by leveraging its stored medical knowledge, the resulting embeddings are likely to be more than just representations of the input string; they could become representations of the *clinical meaning and implications* of that string, leading to more powerful features for prediction.

### **4.4. Discuss the Reported Impact of Input Representation Quality and Prompting Strategies on the Utility of LLM Embeddings in Clinical Contexts**

The utility of LLM-derived embeddings in clinical contexts is significantly influenced by both the quality of the input representation (i.e., how EHR data is serialized) and the prompting strategies employed. These two factors often have a symbiotic relationship: high-quality serialization provides better raw material for the LLM, and effective prompting helps the LLM to make the most of that material.

Impact of Input Representation Quality:  
The method chosen for serializing structured EHR data into a textual format that LLMs can process is foundational.

* Arsanjani et al. (2025) found that converting structured EHR data into markdown-formatted plain text, where medical codes were replaced with their natural language descriptions, was an effective strategy for enabling general-purpose LLMs to generate useful embeddings for clinical prediction.40 This suggests that making the input more akin to natural language, which LLMs are trained on, improves their ability to derive meaningful representations.  
* Conversely, if the input representation is poor—for example, if critical information is lost during serialization, if numerical data is poorly tokenized, or if the textualization is ambiguous—the resulting LLM embeddings are likely to be suboptimal, regardless of the prompting strategy. Studies on LLM-based text summarization, such as the ConTextual framework 93, emphasize the importance of preserving context-specific important tokens and enriching them with structured knowledge, highlighting that input quality and content are paramount.  
* In multimodal contexts, such as the study by Kim et al. (referenced in 127) using GPT-4V for diagnostic tasks with brain MRI scans, the composition of the input prompt (including images, annotations, medical histories, and image descriptions) had a relevant impact on diagnostic performance. Prompts containing all four elements achieved the highest accuracy, and performance declined significantly when crucial elements like image descriptions were omitted.127 This underscores that the richness and completeness of the input representation directly affect the LLM's performance and, by extension, the quality of any derived embeddings.

Impact of Prompting Strategies:  
Prompting strategies have a demonstrable effect on LLM outputs in various medical applications, and this logically extends to the quality of embeddings.

* Numerous studies have shown that thoughtful prompt engineering improves the accuracy, relevance, and coherence of LLM outputs for tasks like clinical information extraction, medical question answering, and the generation of patient education materials.70 For example, using structured prompts or custom instructions led to improved accuracy and comprehensiveness in generating patient education content.70  
* The LLaMA-RAG model's performance in predicting postoperative mortality and ASA classification was significantly enhanced by the use of few-shot prompting and retrieval-augmented generation.78 The F1-score for mortality prediction, for instance, increased dramatically from 0.0436 (with no RAG and no few-shot examples) to 0.4663 (with both RAG and few-shot examples), illustrating the profound impact of prompting context.  
* Agbavor and Liang (referenced in 117) observed that the performance of LLMs in standardizing diagnoses varied significantly depending on the prompt engineering setup, with specific prompt formulations (QWEN prompts) outperforming others.  
* If prompting can improve an LLM's "understanding" or processing of clinical input for direct generation or classification tasks, it is highly probable that it also positively influences the quality of the internal representations (embeddings) derived during that processing. More focused, relevant, and contextually enriched embeddings are likely to lead to better performance in downstream clinical prediction models that use these embeddings as features. The poorer performance of general-purpose LLMs for direct prediction on simply serialized EHR data, as reported by Brown et al. 85, might be partly attributable to the use of basic or sub-optimal prompting; more sophisticated prompting techniques could potentially improve these direct prediction results or, at a minimum, enhance the utility of embeddings derived from such interactions.

The interplay between serialization quality and prompt effectiveness is crucial. A high-quality, semantically rich serialization provides a strong foundation, but effective prompting is needed to help the LLM fully leverage this information. Conversely, even the most sophisticated prompt may not rescue a poor serialization that has obscured or lost vital clinical information. Therefore, achieving optimal LLM-derived embeddings for clinical prediction likely requires a dual focus: developing robust EHR serialization methods that make the data "LLM-friendly" and designing intelligent prompting strategies that direct the LLM's processing of this serialized data towards the specific clinical predictive goals.

## **Section 5: Hybrid Models: Integrating LLM-derived Features with Raw Clinical Data**

The pursuit of enhanced accuracy and robustness in clinical risk prediction has led researchers to explore hybrid modeling approaches. These models aim to combine the strengths of features derived from Large Language Models (LLMs)—such as embeddings from clinical notes or serialized structured data—with traditional structured or raw EHR data. This section reviews the rationale behind developing such hybrid systems, surveys different architectural strategies for fusing these heterogeneous data types, and summarizes findings from studies that have developed and evaluated these models.

### **5.1. Rationale for Developing Hybrid Models in Clinical Prediction**

The primary motivation for developing hybrid models in clinical risk prediction stems from the understanding that different data modalities and feature types within EHRs often capture distinct and complementary aspects of a patient's health status.73 This recognition leads to the hypothesis that integrating these diverse information sources can yield more comprehensive patient representations and, consequently, more accurate and reliable predictive models.

* **Complementary Information Content:** Structured EHR data, such as laboratory values, vital signs, codified diagnoses (e.g., ICD codes), and demographic information, provide quantitative, factual, and standardized data points. LLM-derived features, on the other hand, can capture nuanced information from unstructured clinical narratives (e.g., physician's reasoning, patient-reported symptoms, social context) or learn complex, non-linear relationships from serialized structured data that might be missed by traditional algorithms.80 By combining these, hybrid models aim to create a richer, more holistic view of the patient.  
* **Addressing Limitations of Unimodal Models:** Models that rely exclusively on structured data may fail to leverage the rich contextual information present in clinical notes or other unstructured text.24 Conversely, models based solely on LLM-derived features from text might overlook precise quantitative measurements or structured data elements that are not explicitly mentioned in the narratives. For instance, if LLMs are only applied to clinical notes, they might miss critical lab values or codified diagnoses that are recorded elsewhere in the EHR but not detailed in the notes for a particular encounter. Hybrid models seek to overcome these unimodal limitations.  
* **Potential for Improved Predictive Performance:** The central premise is that by synergistically combining heterogeneous data sources, hybrid models can achieve superior predictive performance (e.g., higher AUROC, AUPRC, or F1-scores) compared to models trained on any single data modality alone.73 Li et al. (2024) explicitly state that their multimodal transformer aims to "boost performance" by fusing clinical notes with structured EHR variables.80  
* **Enhanced Interpretability (Potentially):** While adding complexity, hybrid models can also offer new avenues for interpretability. By analyzing the contributions of different feature types (e.g., structured data components vs. LLM-derived embeddings), it may be possible to gain insights into which aspects of a patient's record are most predictive. The multimodal transformer developed by Li et al. (2024), for example, employed techniques like Integrated Gradients (IG) and Shapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) to explore feature importance from both modalities.80

The development of hybrid models reflects a pragmatic approach in clinical AI: acknowledging that LLMs, while powerful, may not optimally capture all types of signals present in diverse EHR data (especially structured numerical data if not perfectly textualized), and traditional ML methods, while effective for structured data, often neglect unstructured text. Hybridization, therefore, seeks to harness the "best of both worlds" by integrating the semantic understanding and representation power of LLMs with the factual precision of structured data.

### **5.2. Survey of Different Architectural Approaches for Fusing Heterogeneous Data Types**

Several architectural strategies have been reported in the literature for fusing heterogeneous data types, such as LLM-derived features and raw/structured clinical data. These strategies can generally be categorized based on the stage at which fusion occurs: early, intermediate, or late fusion.

* Early Fusion (Input-Level Fusion):  
  In early fusion, data from different modalities are combined at the very beginning of the modeling pipeline, often before being fed into a unified predictive model.108 This can involve:  
  * **Concatenation of Raw Features:** Simple concatenation of feature vectors from different sources. For instance, textualizing structured data elements and appending them to clinical notes to create a single long text sequence for LLM processing.  
  * **Joint Embedding Space:** Learning a common embedding space where features from different modalities can be represented and combined. The systematic review by Teles et al. (2025) notes that early fusion, whether through vectorized representations or data textualization, enables models to learn joint feature representations from the outset.108  
* Intermediate Fusion (Joint Representation Learning):  
  Intermediate fusion involves processing features from different modalities through separate pathways initially, with their learned representations being fused at one or more intermediate layers within a deep neural network architecture.108 This allows for modality-specific feature extraction before integration. Common techniques include:  
  * **Concatenation of Learned Representations:** Feature vectors learned by separate encoders for each modality are concatenated and then fed into subsequent shared layers for joint processing. This approach is seen in the work by Zhang et al. (2024) (HyperFusion), where structural graph embeddings and semantic LLM embeddings are concatenated to initialize node features in a hypergraph.73 The multimodal transformer by Li et al. (2024) also concatenates the output of its main transformer block with the initial notes embedding before the final MLP layer.80  
  * **Attention Mechanisms:** Attention mechanisms can be employed to dynamically weigh the importance of features from different modalities or different elements within each modality when creating a fused representation. This allows the model to learn which information sources are most relevant for a given prediction. The multimodal transformer proposed by Li et al. (2024) inherently uses self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms within its transformer blocks to integrate information from clinical notes and structured time-series variables.80  
  * **Gating Mechanisms:** Adaptive gating mechanisms can control the flow of information from different modalities, allowing the model to learn how much each modality should contribute to the fused representation or the final prediction. Yang & Wu (2021), referenced in the study by Li et al. (2024), implemented Multimodal Adaptation Gate (MAG) techniques for such purposes.80  
  * **Transformer-Based Fusion:** The use of transformer architectures themselves as the fusion mechanism is a sophisticated form of intermediate fusion. As demonstrated by Li et al. (2024) 80, a transformer block can process concatenated or jointly encoded embeddings from different modalities, using its attention layers to learn complex inter-modal relationships and produce a unified multimodal representation.  
* Late Fusion (Decision-Level Fusion):  
  In late fusion, separate predictive models are trained independently for each data modality. The predictions from these individual models are then combined at a later stage to produce a final ensemble prediction.108 Common combination methods include averaging, weighted voting, or using the outputs of the unimodal models as input to a meta-classifier. Late fusion offers modularity and can be advantageous when the modalities have significantly different characteristics, data quality, or when one modality is less reliable than another.108

The choice of fusion strategy is not arbitrary and is an active area of research. It likely depends on factors such as the nature of the data modalities, the complexity of their interactions, the amount of available data for each, and the specific requirements of the downstream clinical prediction task. Sophisticated intermediate fusion techniques, particularly those leveraging attention mechanisms within transformer architectures, are emerging as powerful methods for integrating diverse clinical data.

### **5.3. Summarize Findings from Studies Developing and Evaluating Hybrid Models**

Several studies have developed and evaluated hybrid models that integrate LLM-derived features with other forms of clinical data, generally reporting benefits in terms of predictive performance.

* Li et al. (2024) proposed a novel **multimodal transformer** for in-hospital mortality prediction using the MIMIC-III dataset.80 This model specifically aimed to fuse information from clinical notes (processed by a fine-tuned Clinical BERT, termed MBERT, to generate embeddings) and structured time-series EHR data (17 clinical variables like vital signs and lab results). The architecture involved separate initial processing of notes and variables, followed by a multimodal encoder and a transformer block to create a joint representation. Their hybrid model reportedly outperformed other baseline methods, achieving an AUCPR of 0.538 and an AUCROC of 0.877. A key finding was that domain-adaptive pretraining of Clinical BERT on MIMIC-III notes and subsequent task-adaptive fine-tuning for the mortality prediction task (creating MBERT) improved the quality of the note embeddings and, consequently, the overall model performance. This study serves as a strong example of an intermediate fusion strategy leveraging transformers.  
* Arsanjani et al. (2025) investigated the combination of features from **general-purpose LLM-embedding models with features from a specialized EHR foundation model (CLMBR-T-Base)** across 15 clinical prediction tasks in the EHRSHOT benchmark.40 Their results, presented in tabular form (e.g., Table in 40), generally showed that concatenating embeddings from an LLM (like GTE-Qwen2-7B, processing textualized EHRs) with embeddings from CLMBR-T-Base (trained directly on structured EHRs) led to improved AUROC scores compared to using either model's embeddings alone. For example, in one version of their results for "Operational Outcomes" tasks, GTE-Qwen2-7B alone achieved an AUROC of 0.844, CLMBR-T-Base alone achieved 0.824, while their combination (simple concatenation of embeddings followed by a classifier) reached 0.882.74 This demonstrates a clear benefit from fusing LLM-derived semantic features with features learned by models specialized for structured EHR sequences. This approach treats LLM-derived features as an additional, valuable modality to be integrated with existing representations.  
* Zhang et al. (2024) introduced **HyperFusion**, a framework for clinical risk prediction using hypergraphs.73 While their primary focus is on hypergraph learning, their method involves an early/intermediate fusion step for node feature initialization. They combine structural latent representations (derived from applying the DeepWalk algorithm to an EHR-based graph) with semantic embeddings. These semantic embeddings are generated by applying a GPT text-embedding model (text-embedding-ada-002) to the natural language names of medical concepts. The structural and semantic features are concatenated to form the initial node representations for the hypergraph message-passing process. Although specific comparative results against non-hybrid baselines were not detailed in the provided snippet, the methodology explicitly aims to leverage the complementary nature of graph-derived structural information and LLM-derived semantic information.  
* The systematic review by Teles et al. (2025) on multimodal deep learning for EHR data fusion confirms the prevalence of these strategies.108 They noted that early fusion (via vectorized representations or data textualization) allows models to learn joint representations from the beginning, which can be beneficial if modalities are closely related. Late fusion, by contrast, offers modularity and is suitable when data modalities differ significantly in quality or reliability.

Collectively, these studies suggest a trend towards recognizing the value of hybrid approaches. The findings generally indicate that combining LLM-derived features (often from unstructured text or textualized structured data) with features derived from raw structured EHR data (either directly or via other embedding techniques) can lead to improved predictive performance over models that use only a single type of data or feature. The specific architecture chosen for fusing these diverse data streams plays a crucial role in realizing these benefits. The increasing sophistication of fusion techniques, such as the use of attention-based multimodal transformers, points towards a promising direction for future research in this area.

## **Section 6: Methodologies for Evaluation and Benchmarking in Clinical AI using LLMs**

Rigorous evaluation and standardized benchmarking are paramount for advancing the field of clinical AI, particularly with the introduction of complex models like LLMs and hybrid architectures. This section identifies common benchmark datasets, reviews standard clinical prediction tasks, discusses key performance metrics and their interpretation, covers the importance of robust statistical comparison methods, and touches upon research related to data efficiency.

### **6.1. Common Benchmark Datasets Used in Published Research for Clinical Risk Prediction from EHRs**

The development and validation of clinical risk prediction models, especially those involving LLMs, rely heavily on access to large, well-characterized EHR datasets. Several publicly available and institution-specific datasets are frequently mentioned in the literature:

* **MIMIC (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care):**  
  * **MIMIC-III:** This is one of the most widely utilized datasets in clinical AI research. It is a large, freely available, de-identified database containing comprehensive information from over 40,000 patients admitted to intensive care units (ICUs) at the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center between 2001 and 2012\.4 Data includes demographics, vital signs, laboratory measurements, medications, procedure codes, diagnostic codes, clinical notes (over 2 million), imaging reports, and in-hospital mortality data.17 Its richness and accessibility have made it a cornerstone for developing and benchmarking predictive models. However, a noted limitation is its primary focus on ICU stays, potentially lacking broader longitudinal health data spanning extended periods outside critical care.52  
  * **MIMIC-IV:** This is an updated and expanded version of MIMIC-III, covering patient admissions from 2008 to 2019\.11 It continues to be a vital resource for contemporary research.  
* **eICU Collaborative Research Database:** This dataset aggregates de-identified EHR data from multiple ICUs across the United States, offering a more diverse institutional representation compared to single-center datasets like MIMIC.17  
* **Stanford Longitudinal EHR Datasets (EHRSHOT, INSPECT, MedAlign):** Recognizing the limitations of existing datasets for evaluating models on long-term patient trajectories, Stanford University has developed and released several de-identified longitudinal EHR benchmark datasets.52  
  * **EHRSHOT:** This benchmark is specifically designed to address the "evaluation gap" for tasks requiring long-term patient care histories, such as chronic disease management or multi-visit prediction.52 It includes canonical train/validation/test splits to facilitate standardized model comparison.11  
  * **INSPECT:** This dataset contains paired CT scans and radiology note impressions, facilitating research in multimodal AI.52  
  * **MedAlign:** This dataset offers comprehensive clinical documentation, including various note types, providing a detailed longitudinal view of patient care.52 These datasets are often released in formats like OMOP CDM and the Medical Event Data Standard (MEDS) to promote interoperability and ease of use for machine learning applications.17  
* **UK Biobank:** A large-scale biomedical database and research resource containing in-depth genetic and health information from half a million UK participants. It has been used in studies like the GRASP project for developing and validating risk prediction models.38  
* **FinnGen:** A Finnish biobank initiative combining genome information with digital health care data from Finnish biobanks and health registries, utilized for external validation in studies such as GRASP.38  
* **CPRD (Clinical Practice Research Datalink):** This dataset contains anonymized longitudinal primary care EHR data from a network of general practices in the UK.50 It has been used for pre-training models like BEHRT.  
* **Synthea:** A synthetic patient data generator that can create realistic but artificial EHR data.6 While not real patient data, it can be useful for model development, testing, and situations where access to real data is restricted.  
* **Institution-Specific Datasets:** Many studies also utilize EHR data from their own institutions (e.g., Vanderbilt University Medical Center (VUMC) data used in 85; University of Florida Health data used for GatorTron pre-training 46). While valuable for initial model development, findings from single-institution datasets may have limited generalizability.

The availability of these diverse datasets, particularly large public ones like MIMIC, has been instrumental in advancing research. However, challenges remain regarding data harmonization across different sources and ensuring that benchmark datasets adequately represent diverse patient populations and clinical settings to promote equitable AI development.52

### **6.2. Standard Clinical Prediction Tasks Often Addressed**

Research in clinical risk prediction using EHR data, including studies involving LLMs, commonly focuses on a set of standard predictive tasks. These tasks are often chosen for their clinical relevance and the availability of defined outcomes in EHR datasets:

* **In-hospital Mortality / Mortality Prediction:** Predicting whether a patient will die during their current hospital admission or within a specific timeframe (e.g., 30-day, 1-year mortality) is a very common benchmark task. This is a critical outcome for assessing illness severity and the effectiveness of care.15  
* **Length of Stay (LoS) Prediction:** This involves forecasting the duration of a patient's stay in the hospital or in a specific unit like the ICU.4 Accurate LoS prediction can aid in resource planning and patient flow management.  
* **Hospital Readmission Risk:** Predicting the likelihood that a patient will be readmitted to the hospital within a defined period (commonly 30 days) after discharge is a key quality metric and an area of significant interest for interventions.6  
* **Disease Prediction / Onset:** This broad category includes predicting the future diagnosis of various conditions. Examples from the literature include:  
  * Substance Use Disorder (SUD) and Opioid Use Disorder (OUD).6  
  * Alzheimer's Disease and Related Dementias (ADRD).3  
  * Late-onset preeclampsia.9  
  * Various types of cancer and cardiovascular diseases.2  
  * "Next disease prediction," where the model forecasts any potential future ailment based on past history.38  
* **Sepsis Onset Prediction:** Early identification of sepsis is crucial for timely intervention and improved outcomes.  
* **Acute Care Utilization (ACU):** Predicting the need for acute care services, such as emergency department visits or unplanned hospitalizations, often within a specific timeframe (e.g., 90-day ACU in oncology patients 10).  
* **ICU Bouncebacks:** Forecasting the risk of a patient being readmitted to the ICU shortly after being discharged from it.4  
* **American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) Physical Status Classification:** Predicting a patient's preoperative ASA class, which is a measure of their overall physical health and surgical risk.78  
* **Treatment Efficacy or Response Prediction:** Estimating the likelihood that a patient will respond favorably to a specific treatment or therapy.77

These tasks represent clinically significant problems where accurate predictions can have a direct impact on patient care and healthcare system efficiency.

### **6.3. Key Performance Metrics and Their Relevance and Interpretation**

Evaluating the performance of clinical prediction models requires the use of appropriate metrics that reflect their discriminative ability, calibration, and overall utility in the clinical context. The choice of metric is often influenced by the nature of the prediction task (e.g., binary classification, multi-class classification, regression) and characteristics of the dataset, such as class imbalance.

* **Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC or AUC):** This is one of the most frequently reported metrics for binary classification tasks.3 AUROC measures the model's ability to discriminate between positive and negative cases across all possible classification thresholds. An AUROC of 1.0 represents perfect discrimination, while 0.5 indicates performance no better than random chance. It is a useful measure of overall discriminative power.  
* **Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC or PR AUC):** AUPRC is particularly relevant for tasks with imbalanced datasets, which are common in clinical prediction (e.g., predicting rare diseases or adverse events where the positive class is much smaller than the negative class).4 Unlike AUROC, which can be overly optimistic on imbalanced datasets, AUPRC focuses on the trade-off between precision (the proportion of correctly predicted positive instances among all instances predicted as positive) and recall (the proportion of actual positive instances correctly identified by the model). A higher AUPRC indicates better performance, especially in identifying true positives while minimizing false positives.  
* **F1-Score:** The F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a single measure that balances both.4 It is also useful for imbalanced datasets. For multi-class classification tasks, macro-averaged F1 (unweighted mean of F1-scores for each class) or weighted-averaged F1 (F1-scores weighted by class support) are often used.133  
* **Accuracy:** This is the proportion of all predictions that are correct.3 While intuitive, accuracy can be a misleading metric in the presence of class imbalance. For example, in a dataset where only 1% of patients experience an adverse event, a model that predicts "no event" for all patients would achieve 99% accuracy but would be clinically useless.  
* **Precision (Positive Predictive Value \- PPV), Recall (Sensitivity), and Specificity:** These are fundamental metrics for binary classification:  
  * **Precision:** TP/(TP+FP) – Of those predicted positive, how many actually are?.34  
  * **Recall (Sensitivity):** TP/(TP+FN) – Of those who are actually positive, how many were identified?.34  
  * **Specificity:** TN/(TN+FP) – Of those who are actually negative, how many were identified?.78 Sometimes, recall at a specific precision level (e.g., Recall at 80% Precision, or RP80) is reported to evaluate model performance under conditions where minimizing false positives is critical to avoid issues like alarm fatigue.22  
* **Calibration Metrics (e.g., Brier Score, Calibration Plots, Hosmer-Lemeshow test):** Calibration assesses how well the probabilities predicted by a model align with the actual observed frequencies of the outcome.1 A well-calibrated model that predicts a 20% risk for a group of patients should ideally see approximately 20% of those patients experiencing the event. Poor calibration can be problematic even if a model has good discrimination (high AUROC), especially if predicted probabilities are used to make absolute risk-based decisions (e.g., initiating a treatment if risk exceeds a certain threshold).1 Brown et al. (2024) specifically highlighted that non-fine-tuned LLMs (GPT-3.5, GPT-4) exhibited poor calibration in their clinical prediction tasks compared to traditional ML models.85 The Brier score is a common metric for calibration, measuring the mean squared difference between predicted probabilities and actual outcomes; lower scores are better.  
* **C-statistic (Concordance Index):** For binary outcomes, the C-statistic is equivalent to the AUROC.1 It is also commonly used in survival analysis to evaluate the discriminative ability of time-to-event models, representing the probability that for a randomly selected pair of individuals, the one who experiences the event sooner (or has a higher risk) is assigned a higher predicted risk score by the model.38  
* **Quadratic-Weighted Kappa (QWK):** Used to measure inter-rater agreement for categorical items, QWK can be applied to assess agreement between model predictions and human judgments, especially for ordinal classification tasks (e.g., triage acuity levels).128

The selection of appropriate metrics is crucial for a fair and clinically meaningful evaluation of model performance. Relying on a single metric can be misleading, and a combination of metrics assessing discrimination, calibration, and performance on relevant subgroups (especially in imbalanced datasets) is generally recommended.

### **6.4. Importance of Robust Statistical Comparison Methods**

When comparing the performance of different predictive models, particularly when evaluating novel approaches like LLM-based systems against established methods, it is essential to use robust statistical tests to determine if observed differences in performance metrics are statistically significant or merely due to chance.

* **DeLong's Test for AUROC:** A commonly cited method for comparing the AUROCs of two correlated ROC curves (e.g., when two models are evaluated on the same test dataset) is the DeLong test. This non-parametric test allows for a formal statistical comparison and the calculation of a p-value to assess the significance of the difference in AUROCs. Several studies implicitly or explicitly rely on such statistical comparisons to claim superiority of one model over another.  
* **Confidence Intervals:** Reporting confidence intervals (e.g., 95% CI) for performance metrics (AUROC, AUPRC, F1-score, etc.) is crucial.8 Confidence intervals provide a range of plausible values for the true performance metric and help in assessing the precision of the estimate and the significance of differences between models. If the confidence intervals of two models' performance metrics overlap substantially, the difference may not be statistically significant. Bootstrapping is a common technique used to estimate confidence intervals for various performance metrics, especially when their theoretical distributions are complex.78  
* **Paired Statistical Tests:** When comparing models on the same dataset, paired statistical tests (e.g., paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test for differences in metrics across cross-validation folds or bootstrap samples) can be more powerful than unpaired tests as they account for the correlated nature of the performance estimates.

The use of appropriate statistical methods for comparison ensures that claims about model superiority are evidence-based and helps to avoid drawing erroneous conclusions from observed performance differences that might be within the range of random variability. This is particularly important in the medical field where decisions based on model performance can have significant consequences.

### **6.5. Discussion of Research on Data Efficiency**

Data efficiency refers to a model's ability to achieve good performance with limited amounts of training data. This is a particularly relevant consideration in healthcare, where large, high-quality labeled datasets can be expensive and time-consuming to acquire, or may simply not be available for rare diseases or specific subpopulations.

* **LLMs and Few-Shot Learning:** One of the purported advantages of LLMs, especially large pre-trained foundation models, is their potential for strong performance in low-data or few-shot learning scenarios.25 The extensive knowledge acquired during pre-training on vast general corpora (which may include medical text) is thought to enable these models to generalize to new tasks with only a few examples. Several studies have investigated this:  
  * Agbavor and Liang (2025) (referenced in 25) reported that generative LLMs demonstrated strong performance in low-data settings for COVID-19 severity prediction, outperforming traditional classifiers when training data was scarce.  
  * Wang et al. (2025) explored the few-shot diagnostic performance of LLMs for EHR-based disease prediction by converting structured records into narratives and using various prompting strategies.39 Their EHR-CoAgent framework, designed for few-shot learning, showed decent performance compared to traditional supervised methods.  
  * Arsanjani et al. (2025) focused on the performance of LLM-based embeddings in few-shot settings for clinical prediction tasks from the EHRSHOT benchmark, finding that they can often match or surpass specialized EHR foundation models.40  
  * The study by Liu et al. (2023) (referenced in 77) investigated transformers for clinical prediction and specifically addressed the challenge of few-shot learning in predicting rare disease areas, suggesting that LLMs' in-context learning capability could be vital here.  
* **Med-BERT and Performance on Small Datasets:** The Med-BERT paper by Rasmy et al. (2021) explicitly highlighted that pre-trained Med-BERT embeddings significantly boosted prediction accuracy on tasks with small fine-tuning training sets.81 In some cases, Med-BERT could achieve an AUC comparable to a model trained on a dataset ten times larger when compared to deep learning models without Med-BERT pre-training. This underscores the data efficiency benefits of domain-specific pre-training.  
* **GRASP Methodology and Data Efficiency:** The GRASP model, which uses LLM embeddings of medical concept names, was shown to achieve the same predictive performance as conventional models (like XGBoost) with far less training data, thereby enhancing data efficiency.38 This was particularly evident when training on smaller sample sizes (e.g., N=10,000 to 200,000 individuals), where GRASP significantly outperformed XGBoost.38  
* **Traditional Models and Data Requirements:** Traditional statistical models typically have firm assumptions and may perform adequately with smaller datasets if those assumptions hold. However, more flexible ML models, including many deep learning approaches, are often described as "data-hungry" and may require substantially larger datasets (sometimes 10-fold larger) than traditional statistical models to achieve stable and generalizable performance.18

A Comparative Analysis of Internally-Derived vs. Externally-Informed Representations for Clinical Risk Prediction
0. Introduction
0.1 The Importance of Accurate Clinical Risk Prediction
Accurate clinical risk prediction is crucial for proactive patient management, resource optimization, and improving outcomes.¹ Early identification of high-risk patients enables timely interventions and informs strategic hospital decisions.⁴ Electronic Health Records (EHRs) offer vast, longitudinal data from diverse populations, reflecting real-world clinical practice and enabling the development of robust risk models.¹ According to Goldstein et al., EHR-based risk scores are often developed using data from tens of thousands of individuals, far exceeding the scale of typical trials.¹ Integrating these models into EHRs makes risk assessments available at the point of care, facilitating informed decision-making.¹ Key applications include predicting cardiovascular disease (CVD)², detecting Alzheimer's Disease and Related Dementias (ADRD)³, forecasting preeclampsia⁹, and identifying oncology patients at risk for acute care utilization (ACU).¹⁰
0.2 Challenges in Clinical Risk Prediction with EHR Data
Despite their potential, EHR data present significant challenges in data quality, bias, and model generalizability.
Data Quality and Standardization: As byproducts of routine care, EHR data often contain missing values, inconsistencies, and biases.⁷ A lack of data standardization across and within healthcare systems, compounded by geographic variability in coding, hinders the development and deployment of broadly applicable models.¹ Extracting meaningful patterns from this complex data requires sophisticated methods.
Bias in EHR Data:
Retrospective Analysis Bias: Most EHR studies are retrospective, which can introduce information bias and challenges in accurately capturing the temporal sequence of events, potentially leading to flawed models.⁷
Selection Bias: EHR databases tend to overrepresent sicker individuals who interact more with the healthcare system, potentially leading to models that overestimate risk for the general population.⁷
Irregular Patient Visits: Real-world healthcare utilization involves irregular visit frequencies and follow-up, which complicates consistent data collection and can introduce bias reflecting underlying differences in disease severity or access to care.⁷
Model Generalizability and Calibration: Models developed in specific cohorts may lack generalizability to broader patient populations.¹ For instance, the CHARGE-AF score showed good discrimination in development cohorts but poor calibration when applied to a different institution's EHR data.¹ This highlights the critical need for continuous validation and recalibration of prediction models to ensure their reliability across diverse settings.²
Phenotyping Challenges: Relying on International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes for phenotyping is a significant hurdle, as they often lack the clinical nuance and detail necessary for accurate risk prediction models.¹¹
These challenges create a "data rich, information poor" paradox, where the immense volume of patient data¹ does not easily translate into actionable insights using conventional methods due to its inherent complexity.²,¹ This motivates the exploration of advanced techniques like Large Language Models (LLMs).
0.3 Traditional Machine Learning (ML) Approaches
Various traditional ML models have been widely used for clinical risk prediction with structured EHR data, such as demographics, diagnostic codes, medications, and lab results.⁶ Prominent approaches include:
Logistic Regression (LR): A common baseline model due to its simplicity and interpretability, often used for binary classification.³˒⁹
Support Vector Machines (SVM): An established classification technique used in clinical prediction.⁹
Random Forest (RF): An ensemble method that often shows robust performance³ and has demonstrated superior results in CVD risk prediction.⁸
Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM): Powerful ensemble techniques like XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost frequently achieve high predictive accuracy.³ For instance, XGBoost performed best in predicting acute care use in oncology patients¹⁰, and CatBoost performed well for Length of Stay prediction.¹⁴
Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost): Another boosting algorithm used for tasks like early ADRD prediction.³
Traditional Deep Learning (DL): Earlier DL architectures like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and graph-based models (e.g., GRAM)⁶ have also been applied, with DL models showing high pooled AUCs for CVD risk prediction.⁸
0.4 Limitations of Traditional ML that LLMs Might Address
While successful, traditional ML models have limitations that have prompted research into more advanced methods like LLMs.
Capturing Complex Temporal Dynamics: While more flexible than traditional statistical models¹⁸, many ML methods may not adequately capture the complex longitudinal patterns in EHRs.⁶ LLMs, especially transformer-based ones, are designed to process sequential data and model long-range dependencies, offering an advantage for modeling patient trajectories.¹⁶
Utilizing Unstructured Data: An estimated 80% of clinical information is in unstructured narrative text, which traditional ML models struggle to incorporate.¹² LLMs excel at processing unstructured text, potentially unlocking valuable predictive signals currently underutilized.⁴
Feature Engineering Burden: Traditional ML requires significant, time-consuming feature engineering that demands domain expertise.¹³ LLMs can learn relevant feature representations automatically, with LLM-derived embeddings serving as rich features that can alleviate this manual burden.²⁵
Handling High Dimensionality and Sparsity: EHR data are high-dimensional and sparse, which challenges traditional algorithms.³⁷ LLM embedding techniques can more robustly represent such data by capturing underlying semantic relationships between features.³⁵
Data Scarcity for Specific Tasks: Standard deep learning models demand substantial labeled data to perform well.⁶ Pre-trained LLMs, however, have shown strong performance in low-data or few-shot scenarios, which is valuable when large labeled datasets for specific outcomes are unavailable.²⁵
Integrating Domain Knowledge: Traditional ML pipelines do not easily incorporate external domain knowledge.⁶ LLMs are pre-trained on vast textual corpora, including medical literature, and this embedded knowledge can enhance the understanding of clinical data.⁴
The limitations of traditional ML suggest a potential performance plateau, positioning LLMs as a way to transcend these issues by leveraging their strengths in NLP and representation learning. However, model selection requires considering the specific clinical context.¹ Beyond predictive power, factors like model calibration are critical; a model with excellent discrimination (AUROC) may be clinically unsuitable if it is poorly calibrated and absolute risk estimates are needed for decision-making.¹ This perspective is crucial when evaluating if complex models like LLMs offer a tangible advantage over simpler, traditional methods.
1. Overarching Research Question & Hypotheses
This study will move beyond a simple performance comparison to investigate the fundamental differences, limitations, and potential synergies between two distinct modeling paradigms for clinical risk prediction:
Internally-Derived Numerical Models: Systems that learn exclusively from statistical patterns within a given training dataset (e.g., XGBoost on engineered features).
Externally-Informed Semantic Models: Systems that leverage vast, pre-existing knowledge from external text corpora, accessed via Large Language Model (LLM) embeddings.
Our central hypotheses are:
H1 (The Externally-Informed Representation Hypothesis): An optimized pipeline using LLM embeddings (an externally-informed model) will achieve statistically significant improvement in predictive discrimination (AUROC, AUPRC) over an optimized XGBoost model (an internally-derived model) trained on a robustly engineered numerical feature set from the same source data.
H2 (The Orthogonal Information Hypothesis): This hypothesis is reformulated as a three-part claim, which provides a much stronger and more falsifiable framework:
H2a (Error Discordance): The models will exhibit statistically significant discordance in their predictions.
H2b (Differential Cohort Profiles): The discordance is non-random and driven by clinically interpretable differences in patient features, where the NM excels on statistical complexity and the SM on semantic concepts.
H2c (Synergistic Fusion): A hybrid model's performance lift is not a mere ensembling artifact but is disproportionately driven by resolving these specific, characterized disagreements.
H3 (The Encoding Fidelity Hypothesis): The performance of the semantic system is highly sensitive to its data encoding and prompting. Statistically significant performance differences will be observed when varying: (a) the text serialization format (e.g., verbose vs. dense) and (b) the task-alignment of the LLM prompt.
2. Dataset & Prediction Task
Primary Dataset: MIMIC-Extract dataset.
Prediction Target: In-hospital Mortality (mort_hosp). This is a well-defined, clinically relevant binary classification task.
Data Partitioning: All experiments will strictly adhere to the predefined, fixed training, validation, and test splits provided with the dataset to ensure reproducibility and prevent data leakage.
3. Experimental Arms (The Models)
To rigorously test our hypotheses, we define four distinct modeling systems. These arms are designed to isolate the specific contributions of human-engineered statistical features, corpus-derived semantic representations, and the fundamental format of the clinical data presented to the model.
Model 1 (The Human-Engineered Numerical Baseline):
Architecture: An XGBoost model trained on the bespoke numerical feature vector generated in Phase II. This vector is derived from domain expertise to explicitly calculate statistical summaries like means, standard deviations, and slopes for clinically relevant variable categories. A simpler Logistic Regression model will also be trained for comparison.
Purpose: To establish a powerful performance benchmark representing a traditional, knowledge-driven machine learning approach. It serves as the primary comparator for H1.
Model 2 (The Semantic System Pipeline):
Architecture: A multi-stage pipeline: Text Serializer -> Prompt -> LLM Embedder -> Downstream Classifier (XGBoost and Logistic Regression).
Factorial Design: This arm encompasses the full factorial experiment from Phase III, testing multiple text serialization formats (F1: Uninterpreted, F2: Interpreted, F3: Narrative Summary) and prompting strategies across a suite of embedding models.
Purpose: To systematically investigate H3 (The Encoding Fidelity Hypothesis). The top-performing configuration on the validation set is designated the "Champion Semantic System" for all subsequent hypothesis tests.
Model 3 (The Hybrid Fusion Systems):
Architecture: Two fusion models designed to test for synergy between the numerical and semantic modalities.
3A (Early Fusion): An XGBoost model trained on a single vector concatenating Model 1's features with the Champion Semantic System's embedding.
3B (Late Fusion / Stacking): A Logistic Regression meta-learner trained on the out-of-fold predictions from Model 1 and the Champion Semantic System.
Purpose: To directly evaluate H2c (The Synergistic Fusion Hypothesis).
Model 4 (The Foundational Event-Stream Control):
Architecture: The same LLM embedding pipeline as Model 2 (text-embedding-004 embedder and a downstream classifier).
Input Representation: A Chronological Event-Stream Serialization. For each ICU stay, all recorded events (e.g., labs, vitals) from the source data are extracted. Each event is serialized into a string containing its timestamp, its raw event code, and its numerical value if present (e.g., 2076-02-12 21:14:00 | Heart Rate | 78.0). These event strings are sorted chronologically and concatenated into a single text document representing the patient's timeline.
Purpose: This model serves as the critical control to test the value of higher-level data abstraction. It represents a foundational, "un-opinionated" textual representation of the patient record. By comparing the performance of the Champion Semantic System (Model 2) against this Foundational Event-Stream Control (Model 4), we can rigorously determine if the sophisticated summarization and grouping strategies of F1, F2, and F3 provide a tangible performance benefit over feeding the LLM a complete, time-ordered sequence of raw clinical events. This comparison is the definitive test of the value added by our semantic engineering layer.
4. Step-by-Step Analysis Workflow
Phase I: Domain-Informed Feature Triage and Profiling
1. Establish Source of Truth:
All analysis will originate from a single, pre-processed long-format DataFrame containing the first 24 hours of data for each unique ICU stay (icu_stay_id, feature_name, timestamp, value). All subsequent profiling and rule application will be performed exclusively on the training set to prevent any form of data leakage into the validation or test sets.
2. Execute Programmatic Profiling:
For each unique feature_name in the training set, a "Feature Profile" will be calculated, containing the following statistics:
prevalence: The proportion of ICU stays in which the feature was measured at least once.
count_median: The median number of measurements for that feature among the stays where it was present.
cvr (Coefficient of Variation Ratio): The population-level coefficient of variation (stddev/mean across all measurements) for the feature.
3. Apply the Domain-Informed Hierarchical Sieve:
Each feature will be assigned to one and only one category by passing it through the following hierarchical rules in sequence. Once a feature is categorized, it is removed from the pool available for subsequent rules. This "waterfall" logic ensures that features are classified based on their most dominant clinical and measurement paradigm.
Rule 1 -> Category 0 (Static):
Criterion: The feature_name is present in a pre-defined, manually curated list of static or demographic variables.
List: ['height', 'weight’']
Rationale: This is the most critical first step. These features are not time-series variables. Classifying them based on statistical properties like cvr or count_median is clinically invalid and statistically misleading. They must be isolated first.
Rule 2 -> Category 1 (Event-Driven):
Criterion: prevalence < 0.05.
Rationale: Isolates rare diagnostic tests, procedures, or measurements. For these features, the primary predictive signal is their very occurrence and resulting value, not a temporal trend which is often non-existent or sparse.
Rule 3 -> Category 2 (High-Frequency Physiological):
Criterion: count_median >= 12 (representing measurements taken at least every 2 hours on average).
Rationale: Captures continuously monitored vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure) and invasive monitoring data (central venous pressure). These signals are rich in intra-day volatility, and their short- and long-term trends are of high clinical importance.
Rule 4 -> Category 3 (Labile Lab):
Criterion: 2 <= count_median < 12.
Rationale: Isolates routinely measured labs that reflect the patient's acute metabolic state (e.g., electrolytes, glucose, renal function). These are dynamic but are typically measured on a q12h or daily basis, differentiating them from continuous vitals.
Rule 5 -> Category 4 (Stable Index):
Criterion: count_median < 2 AND cvr < 0.15.
Rationale: Identifies variables that are both infrequently measured and biologically stable within a population (e.g., albumin). For these indices, the most recent absolute value is the key signal, and trend calculations are likely to be noisy and uninformative.
Rule 6 -> Category 5 (Sparse Dynamic):
Criterion: All remaining features.
Rationale: This serves as a well-defined fallback category. It captures variables that are measured sporadically and are too dynamic to be stable indices, but not frequently enough to be considered routine labs (e.g., fibrinogen, cholesterol).
4. Verification, Sensitivity Analysis, and Finalization:
Audit Trail Generation: An output table will be generated documenting every feature, its calculated profile (prevalence, count_median, cvr), the rule that triggered its classification, and its final assigned category.
Clinician-in-the-Loop Review: This audit trail will be manually reviewed by a subject-matter expert to confirm that the categorizations align with clinical practice and intuition. Any major discrepancies (e.g., a known vital sign misclassified) will trigger a re-evaluation of the rule criteria.
Final Blueprint: The final, verified, and sensitivity-checked categorization list will be codified and serve as the immutable blueprint for the bespoke feature engineering in Phase II.

Phase III: Factorial Evaluation of Semantic Representation, Prompting, and Narrative Summarization
Objective
The primary objective of this phase is to systematically deconstruct how Large Language Models process structured clinical data for a predictive task. We will rigorously evaluate three distinct factors: (1) the level of explicit clinical interpretation provided in the input (raw numbers vs. flagged abnormalities), (2) the cognitive framing of the task provided by the prompt, and (3) the value of a high-level narrative summary prepended to the full data.
The experimental design is structured to isolate the main effects and key interactions of these factors. The ultimate goal is to identify the single most performant combination of data representation and prompting, which will be designated the "Champion Semantic System" for all subsequent hypothesis testing.
Step 1: Definition of Controlled Input Representations
We will create three distinct and mutually exclusive textual representations for each patient stay. These representations embody contrasting philosophies of data presentation.
Representation F1 (Uninterpreted Structured Data): This is the baseline exhaustive format. It will group all related measurements (both normal and abnormal, imputed and real) under headers which include the relevant units. Values that have been imputed will be explicitly marked. The model must infer all clinical significance from the raw numerical values.
Representation F2 (Interpreted Structured Data): This format is identical in structure and comprehensiveness to F1. However, for non-imputed values, it will append a programmatically-derived qualitative flag (e.g., "High > 100"). This provides an explicit layer of semantic interpretation on top of the exhaustive data.
Representation F3 (Curated Narrative Summary): This format represents a radically different, "less is more" philosophy. It will consist only of a concise, narrative summary. This summary will be programmatically generated to include basic demographics (age, gender) and a bulleted list of only all the abnormal findings and their values. Crucially, this representation omits all normal values, all imputed values, and the entire detailed structured data report. It is a stand-alone, high-level abstraction of the patient's most critical derangements.
Step 2: Definition of Theoretically-Grounded Prompting Strategies
We will test the previously refined suite of six distinct prompts. Each prompt includes a clause specifying the 24-hour ICU context and is designed to condition the embedding model differently.
P0: Control (Null Prompt): No prompt text is provided.
P1: Task-Specific Prompt: Specifies the goal of predicting in-hospital mortality.
P2: Persona-Driven Prompt: Asks the model to assume the perspective of an ICU physician.
P3: Relational-Focus Prompt: Guides focus toward relationships between systems and temporal evolution.
P4: Acute Dysregulation Prompt: Frames the task as capturing the patient's state of acute physiological dysregulation.
P5: Dominant Pathophysiology Prompt: Guides the model to orient the embedding around the primary clinical problem (e.g., cardiovascular, septic).
Step 3: Experimental Design
The experimental design remains a combination of a factorial study and a comparative study, but the interpretation of the second part is now much sharper.
Part A: The Core 2x6 Factorial Experiment (12 Arms): This part remains unchanged. It is a full factorial design crossing the two exhaustive data formats (F1, F2) with all six prompting strategies (P0-P5). It investigates the value of interpretation and prompting within a "data-rich" paradigm.
Part B: The Narrative Abstraction Experiment (6 Arms): This part now tests a competing representation philosophy.
Design: A comparative study where the stand-alone narrative summary (F3) is tested with each of the six prompting strategies (P0-P5).
Hypothesis: This directly tests whether a token-efficient, curated summary of abnormalities (F3) can achieve predictive performance comparable to, or even superior to, an exhaustive data representation (F1/F2).
Step 4: Execution and Evaluation Protocol
For each of the 18 experimental arms, the specified prompt will be prepended to the specified representation format for every patient in the training, validation, and test sets. These complete text inputs will be fed to the LLM embedding model to generate 18 corresponding sets of embedding vectors. For each of the 18 embedding sets, the designated downstream classifiers will be trained and independently hyperparameter-tuned using the training and validation sets. Final performance for each condition will be measured on the held-out test set, using AUROC, AUPRC, and Brier Score.
Step 5: Analysis and Champion Selection
The analysis plan is enhanced by this change, allowing for a new primary comparison.
Champion Selection: The process remains the same: the top-performing of the 18 arms on the validation set is selected.
Analysis of Part A: This analysis remains the same, focused on the main and interaction effects of interpretation and prompting for exhaustive data.
Analysis of Part B: The analysis now focuses on a head-to-head comparison of philosophies. For each prompt, we will compare the performance of the three representations: F1 vs. F2 vs. F3. This will allow us to answer a key question: "For a given cognitive framing (e.g., the Persona-Driven Prompt P2), is it better to provide uninterpreted exhaustive data, interpreted exhaustive data, or a curated narrative summary?" This three-way comparison will be the most insightful output of Phase III.

Phase IV (Final Definitive Protocol): Mechanistic Discordance Analysis
Overarching Objective: To conduct a granular, post-hoc, and statistically robust investigation into the previously observed performance of the final, trained Numerical Model (NM) and Semantic Model (SM). The sole purpose of this phase is to explain the models' differential behavior by discovering, validating, and interpreting the specific clinical archetypes that define their disagreements, thereby testing the Orthogonal Information Hypothesis (H2).
Required Inputs:
Final, Calibrated Models: NM_calibrated and SM_calibrated, which have been fully trained on the combined train + validation dataset.
Complete Partitioned Datasets:
full_training_set: The combined train + validation data.
test_set: The final, held-out test data.
Both sets must contain the low-level numerical features and the ground truth labels (in_hospital_mortality).
Phenotype Blueprint: The pre-defined, harmonized CSV or configuration file detailing the logic for over 60 high-level clinical phenotypes.

Execution Protocol
Part A: Prerequisite Definition & Cohort Construction
Stage A.1: Optimal Threshold Determination on the Full Training Set
Objective: To establish a consistent, statistically optimal classification threshold for each model to enable a fair and direct comparison of their binary disagreements.
Rationale: While the models have already been trained on this data, a threshold is a property of the model's output distribution, not a trained parameter. Determining it on the full training set provides the most stable estimate for defining the cohorts for the subsequent discordance analysis.
Procedure:
Generate Calibrated Training Probabilities: Use the final NM_calibrated and SM_calibrated models to generate calibrated probability predictions for the entire full_training_set.
Optimize for Youden's J Statistic: For each model independently:
Iterate through thresholds from 0.01 to 0.99.
For each threshold, calculate Sensitivity and Specificity on the full_training_set.
Identify the threshold that maximizes J = Sensitivity + Specificity - 1.
Finalize Thresholds: Store these as fixed constants, thresh_NM and thresh_SM.
Stage A.2: Foundational Clinical Feature Engineering
Objective: To transform the low-level numerical features into the high-level, curated set of clinically interpretable concepts required for the analysis.
Procedure:
Input Data: The low-level feature matrices for the full_training_set and test_set.
Execution: Run the dependency-aware script that programmatically parses the Phenotype Blueprint to generate each phenotype (e.g., has_shock_sepsis, aki_severity_stage).
Output: Two DataFrames: X_phenotypes_train and X_phenotypes_test. These are the exclusive feature sets for subgroup discovery.
Stage A.3: Definition of the Eight Analytical Cohorts on the Training Set
Objective: To apply the newly defined thresholds to the training data to construct the precise patient populations for the eight subgroup discovery analyses.
Procedure:
Generate Binary Predictions: Apply thresh_NM and thresh_SM to the calibrated probabilities of the full_training_set.
Define Core Groups: Categorize all patients in the full_training_set into TP, TN, FP, FN for each model, then define concordant (TP_concordant, TN_concordant) and discordant (SM_Win, NM_Win) groups.
Construct Eight Analytical Datasets: Define the precise Population and Target for each of the eight analyses as specified in your original plan (four for Independent Failure Modes, four for Competitive Advantage Battlegrounds).
Part B (Corrected & Definitive): Subgroup Discovery, Validation, and Archetype Selection
Stage B.0: Explicit Cohort and Target Definition
Objective: To create the eight distinct, unambiguous analytical datasets on which the discovery algorithm will be run. All definitions and cohorts in this stage are constructed using the full_training_set only.
Procedure:
Generate Training Set Predictions: Use the final NM_calibrated and SM_calibrated models and the fixed thresholds (thresh_NM, thresh_SM) to generate binary predictions for every patient in the full_training_set.


Define Core Outcome Groups: Using the binary predictions and the true labels, categorize each patient in the full_training_set into one of the following mutually exclusive groups:


TP_concordant: Patients correctly predicted as positive by both models.
TN_concordant: Patients correctly predicted as negative by both models.
SM_Win: Patients where the SM was correct and the NM was incorrect. This includes:
SM True Positives where NM had a False Negative.
SM True Negatives where NM had a False Positive.
NM_Win: Patients where the NM was correct and the SM was incorrect.
Construct the Eight Analytical Datasets:

 Sub-Part 1: Independent Failure Modes (4 Analyses)


Analysis 1 (SM False Negatives):
Population: All patients from FN_SM ∪ TP_concordant.
Target: A binary variable is_SM_miss, which is True for FN_SM patients.
Analysis 2 (SM False Positives):
Population: All patients from FP_SM ∪ TN_concordant.
Target: A binary variable is_SM_false_alarm, which is True for FP_SM patients.
Analysis 3 (NM False Negatives):
Population: All patients from FN_NM ∪ TP_concordant.
Target: A binary variable is_NM_miss, which is True for FN_NM patients.
Analysis 4 (NM False Positives):
Population: All patients from FP_NM ∪ TN_concordant.
Target: A binary variable is_NM_false_alarm, which is True for FP_NM patients.
Sub-Part 2: Competitive Advantage Battlegrounds (4 Analyses)


Analysis 5 (SM Advantage on Deceased):
Population: All deceased patients (true label = 1) from SM_Win ∪ NM_Win.
Target: A binary variable is_SM_win.
Analysis 6 (NM Advantage on Deceased):
Population: Same as Analysis 5.
Target: A binary variable is_NM_win.
Analysis 7 (SM Advantage on Survivors):
Population: All surviving patients (true label = 0) from SM_Win ∪ NM_Win.
Target: A binary variable is_SM_win.
Analysis 8 (NM Advantage on Survivors):
Population: Same as Analysis 7.
Target: A binary variable is_NM_win.
Stage B.1: Exploratory Subgroup Discovery (on full_training_set)
Objective: To generate a comprehensive list of candidate rules from the curated clinical phenotype space.
Procedure:
Construct Restricted Search Space: Using X_phenotypes_train, create a search space containing selectors only for the presence of clinical abnormalities.
Execute Discovery: For each of the eight analytical datasets defined in Stage B.0, run the discovery algorithm (pysubgroup) with exploratory settings (SUBGROUP_MIN_SUPPORT = 0.01, etc.) and multiple depths (max_depth = 2, 3, 4).
Consolidate: Combine all discovered rules into a single master list of candidates.
Stage B.2: Rigorous Statistical and Generalizability Pruning
Objective: To filter the candidate rules, retaining only those that are statistically significant and demonstrate potential to generalize.
Procedure:
Statistical Pruning (on full_training_set): For every candidate rule, calculate a Fisher's Exact Test p-value based on its performance within its respective training cohort. Apply Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction. Discard any rule with q > 0.05.
Generalizability Check (using test_set): For each rule surviving FDR, calculate its Lift on both the full_training_set and the corresponding test_set cohort. Discard any rule where Lift_test shows a severe degradation (e.g., collapses towards 1.0 or is < 70% of Lift_train). This step is a simple check, not a selection or ranking process.
Stage B.3: Systematic Archetype Selection (on full_training_set)
Objective: To distill the filtered list of rules into a final, small, non-redundant set of interpretable archetypes.
Critical Rationale: To avoid data leakage and ensure our selection process is not biased by the specific quirks of the test set, all quantitative pruning, clustering, and final selection decisions must be made using metrics calculated on the full_training_set only.
Procedure (performed independently for the rules from each of the eight analyses):
Quantitative Pruning (on full_training_set): From the list of generalizable rules, keep only those with strong, impactful signals as measured on the training set:
Lift > 2.0 (calculated on the full_training_set)
Coverage > 25 patients (calculated on the full_training_set)
Redundancy Clustering (on full_training_set): For each remaining rule, calculate the set of patient_IDs it covers in the full_training_set. Cluster rules where their patient sets have a Jaccard Similarity > 0.85.
Archetype Selection (based on full_training_set metrics): From each cluster, select the single best representative rule using the explicit decision hierarchy, where all metrics are calculated on the training set:
Priority 1: Parsimony & Interpretability (clearest clinical story).
Priority 2 (Tie-breaker): Highest Lift (on full_training_set).
Priority 3 (Tie-breaker): Highest Coverage (on full_training_set).
Stage B.4: Final Unbiased Reporting (on test_set)
Objective: To report the final, honest performance of the selected archetypes on unseen data.
Procedure:
Take the final, curated list of archetypes selected in Stage B.3.
For each archetype, apply its rule to the appropriate test_set cohort.
Calculate and report the final performance metrics (Coverage, Lift, WRAcc, Subgroup Target Share, etc.) for each archetype as measured on the test set. These are the final, unbiased results that will be presented in your analysis.
Part C: Final Synthesis and Hypothesis Conclusion
Stage C.1: Final Reporting
Objective: To synthesize all findings into a cohesive narrative that explains the models' differential performance.
Procedure:
Present Archetype Analysis: The primary output will be two summary tables for the archetypes selected in Stage B.3:
Table 1 (Independent Failure Modes): Showcasing the archetypes from Analyses 1-4.
Table 2 (Competitive Advantages): Structured into four sections, presenting the distinct archetypes from Analyses 5-8.
For each archetype, report the rule definition, the analysis it belongs to, and its final test set statistics (Coverage, Lift, q-value).
Hypothesis Conclusion (H2b): The report will focus on the clinical interpretation of these archetypes as direct evidence for or against the Orthogonal Information Hypothesis. A successful outcome is the generation of non-trivial, statistically robust, and clinically plausible archetypes across the eight analyses.

Phase V: Meta-Analysis of Data Structure within Discovered Failure Phenotypes
Objective: Having identified the clinical phenotypes of failure in Phase IV, this phase investigates the underlying data-architectural reasons for those failures. It directly tests the hypotheses that SM failures are linked to data density, volatility, or imputation burden.
New Hypotheses (More Precise):
H_meta_1 (Data Density): The Clinical Failure Phenotypes where the SM fails will exhibit statistically significant differences in data density metrics (total_measurement_count, input_token_count) compared to concordant success cohorts.
H_meta_2 (Volatility Blindness): The Clinical Failure Phenotypes where the SM fails will exhibit statistically significant differences in volatility metrics (aggregate_stddev, aggregate_slope) compared to concordant success cohorts.
H_meta_3 (Imputation Burden): The Clinical Failure Phenotypes where the SM fails will exhibit statistically significant differences in imputation metrics (imputation_proportion) compared to concordant success cohorts.
Step 1: Meta-Feature Calculation
This step remains unchanged. For every patient in the test set, calculate the full suite of meta-features:
Density: total_measurement_count, unique_feature_count, input_token_count.
Volatility: aggregate_stddev, aggregate_slope.
Imputation: total_imputation_count, imputation_proportion.
Step 2: Linking Clinical Phenotypes to Data Characteristics (Primary Meta-Analysis)
Rationale: This is the crucial link. We are no longer comparing all FN_SM patients to all TP_concordant patients. Instead, we are analyzing the data characteristics of the specific, high-quality subgroups discovered in Phase IV.
Procedure:
Take a top-ranked, clinically interpretable subgroup from Phase IV. For example, let's call it S1, the SM False Negative subgroup defined by the rule (slope_lactate_24h > 0.2) AND (GCS_last > 13).
For each meta-feature (e.g., aggregate_stddev), perform a Mann-Whitney U test comparing its distribution for patients within subgroup S1 against its distribution for patients in the corresponding success cohort (TP_concordant).
Repeat this for every meta-feature and for every high-quality subgroup discovered in Phase IV for both false negatives and false positives.
Interpretation: This allows for highly specific conclusions.
Example Finding: "For the SM failure subgroup characterized by rising lactate in neurologically intact patients, we found that the aggregate_stddev (p < 0.01) and input_token_count (p < 0.05) were significantly higher than in the concordant true positive cohort. This suggests that while the NM could isolate the critical lactate trend, the SM was overwhelmed by a high volume of noisy, albeit non-critical, physiological data, leading it to miss the key signal."
Step 3: Exploratory Subgroup Discovery with Combined Feature Sets
Rationale: Now that we have established the primary clinical failure modes and their data-architectural correlates, we can perform the analysis you suggested as a final, exploratory step to uncover complex interactions.
Procedure: Repeat the four Subgroup Discovery analyses from Phase IV, but this time, the search space will be the union of the clinical features AND the meta-features.
Purpose: To discover novel rules that might have been missed, especially those that explicitly combine clinical and data-structural properties.
Example of a Potential Novel Finding: A rule might emerge like (creatinine_last > 3.0) AND (imputation_proportion > 0.6). This has a powerful clinical interpretation: "The SM is particularly prone to false alarms in patients with both high creatinine and a large amount of missing data elsewhere, suggesting it misinterprets the imputed 'normal' values as a sign of stability that contradicts the high creatinine, creating a confusing signal." This finding would be distinct from and complementary to the primary analysis.
Step 4: Final Synthesis & Reporting
The analysis will be reported sequentially.
Phase IV Results: Present the table of Clinical Failure Phenotypes (the rules from SD on clinical features) as the primary finding regarding H2.
Phase V Results: Present a second table showing the meta-analytic results for each of those phenotypes, linking them to data characteristics.
Exploratory Findings: Discuss any novel interactive rules from Step 3 of Phase V as supplementary, hypothesis-generating findings.
This structured reporting provides a clear, logical, and compelling narrative that builds from a high-level performance comparison down to a nuanced, mechanistic explanation of model failure.
5. Evaluation Metrics & Statistical Analysis
Primary Discrimination Metrics: Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC), Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC).
Primary Calibration Metric: Brier Score.
Visualizations: Calibration Plots, Reliability Diagrams, Data Efficiency Curves.
Statistical Significance: All AUROC comparisons will be made using the DeLong test. All reported metrics will include 95% confidence intervals. The threshold for statistical significance will be p < 0.05.
6. Final Synthesis & Reporting
The final report will be structured to sequentially address H1-H4. It will synthesize quantitative results (master performance table), qualitative findings (error analysis), and mechanistic explanations (network analysis) into a cohesive narrative. A prominent "Limitations" section will explicitly discuss the black-box nature of the LLM, the specific choices made in feature engineering and serialization, and the implications of the control experiment's findings.
